Why in-person work is not the only way to build culture

RTO advocates also claim that physical presence builds culture. Culture is built through everything we do – in person or online, through both spoken and written words. The real question is what kind of culture we build. Top-down RTO mandates are more likely to communicate cultures where control is more important than trust, where obedience is more important than autonomy, and where presence is more important than results. Instead of mandates, focus on interactions and activities that reinforce the values you want to see in your culture.

Remote-and-connected WFH days can be filled with virtual meetings for tasks that don’t require in-person exchange. These tasks are often familiar or autonomous, such as routine tasks, offering consulting or being consulted on expertise, and decision-making via established processes.

For cognitively demanding tasks that require deep work, uninterrupted time is critical. These tasks can be completed anywhere if the environment minimizes distractions. Shallow tasks, such as routine work with low task autonomy or a lot of repetition, can also be done anywhere, especially if employees have the necessary skills, expertise, and resources.

Deep focus and concentration in the office

getty

During deep and shallow work, individual productivity is high: employees crack complex problems, complete tasks, and move forward with their to-do lists. However, clear outcome targets must be set, and managers should create a culture where help is readily offered and reaching out for help is encouraged. Mentoring, coaching, and regular touchpoints ensure bottlenecks are removed quickly.
