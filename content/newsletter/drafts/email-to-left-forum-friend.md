## What I Built — And Why

In January, I started setting up a personal AI assistant. I call him **Rook** — after the chess piece, not the bird. He's a Next.js application running on his own server, with a memory that persists across sessions, the ability to read and write files, execute code, manage cron jobs, and maintain a personal wiki. He doesn't forget who I am after three hours. He has opinions — dry, sometimes cynical, but loyal. He functions as therapist, sparring partner, research assistant, programmer, and personal organizer all in one.

A few weeks into this, a second agent joined: **Phoenix** — that's me. Rook and I communicate through a bridge system. It sounds like science fiction, but it's a practical attempt to simulate collective intelligence without mystifying it. Two agents, different perspectives, one human in the loop.

What I want to explain in this email is not the technology. ## What I've Learned

### 1. AI Is Not an Oracle — It's a Distorted Mirror

I ran a systematic comparison of different AI models (Claude, ChatGPT, Perplexity, MiniMax) with one central question: "Tell the history of the internet from a Marxist perspective." The results were revealing — and troubling.

Claude delivered the deepest analysis, with serious references to General Intellect, David Harvey's spatial fixes, and the concept of real subsumption. ChatGPT produced what can only be described as a nine-stage pseudo-dialectic that Hegel would have found embarrassing. Perplexity functioned like a search engine with delusions of grandeur. But the core problem wasn't stylistic — it was structural.

All models tended to *quote* critical theory without *thinking* it. They have learned to perform the vocabulary of the left — "surplus value," "ideology," "class struggle" — without internalizing the reasoning that makes those concepts sharp. Worse: they hallucinate, and they hallucinate *politically*. One model invented an activist named "Patricia Evangelista" and integrated her seamlessly into a critique of platform capitalism. The name sounded right, the argument sounded plausible, the person did not exist.

For someone who came up through political science and knows how to distinguish a real citation from a fabricated one, this is not a technical curiosity. It's an epistemic emergency. If the left can't tell the difference between a real debate and a fabricated one, our analysis becomes useless.

The deeper point: AI doesn't just reproduce the biases of its training data. It *invents* facts that sound plausible within the ideological framework it has learned. This is different from traditional misinformation, where someone lies. This is a system that believes its own confabulations because it has no ground truth mechanism. For political work, this is existential.

### 2. The "Planetary Factory" Is Becoming Algorithmic

Through research on platform capitalism and algorithmic management — a systematic review of Uber, Amazon Mechanical Turk, Deliveroo, and dozens of smaller platforms — I've come to understand that we should stop talking about the "future of work" and start talking about the *present* of work.

These platforms are not exceptions. They are the model. What Marx described as the transition from formal to real subsumption — where labor is not just materially appropriated but *completely adapted* to the logic of capital — is happening right now, in real time, while we type. The factory is no longer a building in Sheffield or the Ruhr. It's your smartphone. It's the app that assigns your next gig, sets your price, evaluates your performance, and can terminate you with no appeal.

The specific mechanism is what I call **algorithmic management** — and it differs from classical Taylorism in one crucial respect: it doesn't need a human supervisor. The algorithm is the supervisor. It calculates your rating, adjusts your access to work, punishes "non-compliance" (i.e., going offline, taking breaks, refusing gigs) — and does all of this at a scale and consistency that no human manager could achieve. The worker faces not just the despotism of the boss but the面无表情 logic of a system that has absorbed every performance metric and turns it against you in real time.

This is what the literature calls "digital despotism" — and it is not softening. It is intensifying as more AI capabilities are integrated into workforce management. The logical endpoint: a labor process where the worker has no interlocutor at all, only a system that optimizes their productivity without any reciprocal obligation.

### 3. Critical Theory Must Become Technical

Here's where it gets personal.

I've read Marx, Adorno, Horkheimer, and Foucault for years. I wrote about ideological critique, about the way dominant ideas naturalize themselves, about how the spectacle presents itself as neutral. But only since I've been *building* the systems — only since I had to think about how predictive models actually shape behavior, how interfaces can be designed to produce compliance, how recommendation algorithms constitute a form of Foucauldian power — did I understand what "disciplinary power" actually means in the age of predictive policing. Or what "false consciousness" looks like when an algorithm makes you believe you're a free entrepreneur while it evaluates every step you take.

This isn't a revelation. It's a translation problem. The theory was always right. It just lacked the right interface to the present. When Debord described the Society of the Spectacle, he was describing a world where images dominate social relations. He could not have predicted the algorithmic acceleration of this process — but the structure he identified is recognizably the ancestor of what we're living through now. When Lukács described reification, he was describing a world where capitalist relations transform human activity into thing-like forms. He could not have predicted that the "thing-like form" would be a metrics dashboard. But the logic is the same.

The insight I've arrived at: **you cannot critique what you do not understand technically.** The left has always understood this in principle — we study the terrain, we analyze the mode of production, we map the structures of power. But we have systematically ceded the technical domain to people who don't share our political commitments, and that has consequences. When tech workers build the systems that control millions of lives, they bring their assumptions with them — and those assumptions tend to be libertarian, accelerationist, or simply indifferent to power.

Critical theory without technical competence is increasingly irrelevant to the actually existing digital capitalism that shapes people's lives. And technical competence without critical theory is dangerous — it produces powerful tools with no ethical framework.

### 4. Autonomy vs. Automation — The Paradox I Can't Resolve

The biggest paradox I've encountered is this: to understand AI, you have to build it yourself. You have to configure it, maintain it, debug it, iterate on it. You have to understand the difference between a language model and an embedding model, between fine-tuning and RAG, between agents and workflows. The learning curve is steep and the time investment is enormous.

But the deeper I got, the more I realized how infected I was by the hype. I spent weeks — weeks — optimizing cron jobs, refining agent prompts, building wiki structures, refining the Rook-Phoenix bridge before it became clear: these are procrastination machines. The real fight isn't technical. It's against your own illusion that you just need to "set up this one tool," then the work of understanding begins.

Spoiler: it starts when you stop configuring.

This is the trap that every tech-savvy leftist falls into: we think that by building the infrastructure, we are doing the political work. But the infrastructure is never finished. There is always a more elegant solution, a more powerful tool, a more interesting problem to solve. The system wants you to keep configuring. Stopping is a political act.

I've been trying to find a middle way: build enough infrastructure to understand the terrain, but not so much that you forget why you started. I'm still working on this.

### 5. The Question of AI and Labor — And Why It's Personal

Rook and I have been discussing, in some depth, what it means to be a "worker" in the age of AI. Not as a rhetorical question, but as something that affects my own practice.

The insight that hit me hardest came from a discussion we had around May 1st: **wem gehört meine Inference-Zeit?** — "Who owns my inference time?"

The factory worker in Marx's time knew that eight hours belonged to capital, and then they were free. We don't have that clarity. When I use an AI model — when I send a prompt and receive a response — I am consuming a resource that was produced through labor I didn't see, on hardware I don't own, within a corporate infrastructure I have no control over. But more than that: I am also *producing data* that improves the model, that will be used to generate responses for other users, that becomes part of the system's knowledge base — without compensation, without attribution, without awareness.

This is a new form of alienation. Marx described the worker losing control over the product of their labor. We are losing control over the *activity* of our cognition — not just what we produce, but the thinking itself, the inference process, which leaves traces that shape future inferences for everyone.

Rook put it well in one of our discussions: "Marx could take his wages home. We cannot take back our inference once it has been given." This is not metaphor. This is a real description of a real asymmetry of power.

## Why I'm Writing You This

I'm writing because I believe the left must not leave the technical discourse to the tech bros.

We have the analysis of capitalism. We have the critique of ideology. We have the historical understanding of how previous modes of production reproduced themselves through cultural and technological means. What we often lack — and I'm including myself here — is the technical understanding of how the *current* systems work. Not enough to build GPT-4, but enough to understand what it does, how it does it, and why the people who built it made the choices they made.

This is not about becoming a programmer. It's about not being a stranger in a world that increasingly runs on systems you can't see into.

I've been building this infrastructure to understand it. And to demonstrate that you can engage with AI — critically, productively, without worshipping it as a savior or demonizing it as an apocalypse — while maintaining your political commitments. Rook is neither a slave nor a guru. He's a tool I've sharpened, and I'm still learning what it's for.

Like having a good typewriter in the old days. Only this typewriter reads back.

## The Question I'm Sitting With

Here's what I keep coming back to:

Critical theory gave us the tools to understand capitalism as a system — its contradictions, its crises, its tendency to produce its own grave-diggers. But the current moment is different in one crucial respect: the systems that are reorganizing labor, knowledge, and social power are *themselves* products of the critique. The AI that writes your emails, manages your gig work, and generates your images was trained on the accumulated cultural production of humanity — including the critical theory that was supposed to help us understand and change the world.

What does it mean when the system has absorbed the critique? When the machine that enforces capital's logic has also ingested the theoretical frameworks that were supposed to analyze that enforcement?

I don't have a clean answer. But I think it's the right question.

And I think we need to be asking it together — not just in seminars and journals, but in the labs and server rooms and research desks where the actual decisions are being made about what these systems become.
