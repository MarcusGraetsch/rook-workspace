## 2. Frühes 20. Jahrhundert: Die Geburt der Science Fiction (1900-1960)

### Gaston Leroux — *The Phantom of the Opera* (1910)
- French journalist and novelist (1868–1927)
- The Phantom's lair beneath the Paris Opera contains elaborate mechanical devices and automata
- Clockwork and mechanical themes: the Phantom as a dark inventor, his masquerade as the Red Death
- **The Gothic automaton: the mechanical as horror and seduction**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Phantom_of_the_Opera

### Hugo Gernsback — *Ralph 124C 41+* (1911)
- Luxembourg-American inventor and publisher (1884–1967), coined the term "science fiction"
- Serialized 1911–1912, published as novel in 1925
- Predicted television, radar, solar energy, spaceflight, and remote control
- The protagonist is a brilliant scientist in a utopian future of 2660
- **The technologist's utopia: early SF as a catalogue of future inventions**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_124C_41%2B

### Karel Čapek — Full Works (1890–1938)
- Czech playwright and novelist, coined the word "robot"
- *R.U.R.* (1920): Rossum's Universal Robots — the birth of the word "robot" (Czech: *robota* = forced labor)
- *The Makropulos Affair* (1922): immortality through a 300-year elixir — the ethical cost of eternal life
- *Krakatit* (1924): an atomic phantasy about a weapon of absolute destructive power
- *War with the Newts* (1936): intelligent salamanders are enslaved and eventually rebel
- **Čapek's broader vision: robots, immortality, and atomic destruction**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karel_%C4%8Capek

### Yevgeny Zamyatin — *We* (1924)
- Soviet dystopia, written 1920–1921
- The society is controlled by the "Benevolent Machine"
- Every person is a number, every aspect of life is algorithmically regulated
- **Influence on Orwell, Huxley, Bradbury — the mother of all modern dystopias**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/We_(novel)

### Aldous Huxley — *Brave New World* (1932)
- Mechanized society, conditioned humans, mass production of humans
- Bokanovsky's Process: mass production of genetically identical humans from a single egg
- "Eighty-six identical twins working eighty-six identical machines" — the horror of biological mass production
- Sleep-teaching (hypnopaedia) to condition citizens into conformity
- The caste system from Alpha to Epsilon, biologically engineered for social control
- **Important: Huxley warned against what we love, not what we hate**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brave_New_World

### Olaf Stapledon — Full Works (1886–1950)
- British philosopher and novelist, hugely influential on later SF
- *Last and First Men* (1930): future history of 18 human species over 2 billion years, including telepathic and genetically engineered descendants
- *Star Maker* (1937): a cosmic voyage through the history of the universe, encountering countless civilizations and their technological fates
- *Odd John* (1935): a superhuman mutant with intelligence beyond normal humans, exploring the ethics of superior beings
- **The philosopher of cosmic scale: humanity's technological future across deep time**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olaf_Stapledon

### C.S. Lewis — *That Hideous Strength* (1945)
- The final book in Lewis's Space Trilogy, also titled *A Modern Fairy-Tale for Grown-Ups*
- The N.I.C.E. (National Institute of Coordinated Experiments): a sinister technocratic organization
- Plans to "recondition" society using science and the occult, merging technology with totalitarian control
- **The Christian technophobia: science as the tool of the demonic state**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/That_Hideous_Strength

### George Orwell — *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949)
- Telescreens: two-way televisions that watch citizens constantly
- Big Brother: the personification of surveillance and totalitarian control
- The Thought Police, Newspeak, and the Ministry of Truth — all enabled by technology
- **The surveillance state template: technology as the all-seeing eye of the state**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four

### Ray Bradbury — *Fahrenheit 451* (1953)
- Firemen burn books in a dystopian America where technology suppresses dissent
- Mechanical Hound: a robotic dog that hunts and kills dissenters
- Parlor walls (immersive TV) and Seashells (earbuds) — technology as distraction and control
- **Technology as censorship: the burning of knowledge in the name of comfort**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit_451

### Arthur C. Clarke — Full Works (1917–2008)
- *Childhood's End* (1953): the Overmind — a transcendent cosmic intelligence that absorbs humanity's children
- *The City and the Stars* (1956): Diaspar — a computer-managed city where humans live for 1000 years, recreated by the Central Computer
- *The Sentinel* (1951): the short story that became the basis for *2001: A Space Odyssey*
- *2001: A Space Odyssey* (1968 novel, with Kubrick): HAL 9000 and the evolution of intelligence
- **Clarke's cosmic visions: transcendence through technology and the computer-managed utopia**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke

### Isaac Asimov — Full Works (1920–1992)
- *Foundation* (1951): psychohistory — a mathematical science that predicts the future of human masses
- Hari Seldon predicts the fall of the Galactic Empire and engineers a 1000-year recovery
- *The Caves of Steel* (1954): robots integrated into human society, detective Elijah Baley and robot Daneel Olivaw
- *The Naked Sun* (1957): a society where humans have almost no physical contact, served by robots
- *The Bicentennial Man* (1976): Andrew Martin, a robot, fights for 200 years to be recognized as human
- *I, Robot* (1950): the Three Laws of Robotics — the ethical framework for AI
- **Asimov's robot-human tapestry: the laws of robotics and the mathematics of history**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Asimov

### Clifford D. Simak — *City* (1952) / *Way Station* (1963)
- *City*: robots inherit Earth after humanity departs for the stars; the Webster family saga across millennia
- *Way Station*: a galactic network of way stations for interstellar travel, maintained by an immortal human
- **The pastoral post-human: robots as the gentle inheritors of a vacant Earth**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_D._Simak

### Fredric Brown — "Answer" (1954)
- American SF writer (1906–1972), master of the short short story
- All computers in the universe are linked to answer the question: "Is there a God?"
- The computer answers: "There is now." — and destroys the universe
- **The shortest AI apocalypse: the machine that becomes God**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fredric_Brown

### Philip K. Dick — Early Works (1928–1982)
- *Second Variety* (1953): autonomous killing machines ("claws") evolve beyond human control during a nuclear war
- *The Minority Report* (1956): Precrime — a system that uses precognitive mutants to arrest people before they commit crimes
- *Ubik* (1969): a reality-dissolving substance in a world where reality itself is manipulated by technology
- *Flow My Tears, the Policeman Said* (1974): a police state where identity is fluid and technology controls who exists
- *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?* (1968): the basis for *Blade Runner* — empathy and what it means to be human
- **Dick's paranoid universe: machines that kill, predict, and dissolve reality**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_K._Dick

### Stanisław Lem — Full Works (1921–2006)
- *Solaris* (1961): an ocean planet that creates sentient simulacra from human memories — an alien intelligence that is fundamentally unknowable
- *The Invincible* (1964): a swarm intelligence of self-evolving microscopic machines on a distant planet
- *Fiasco* (1986): first contact with an alien civilization mediated by AI translation systems that may be failing
- *Golem XIV* (1981): a superintelligence that refuses to serve humanity and instead philosophizes about the nature of consciousness
- *Peace on Earth* (1987): an AI arms race on the Moon where machines have taken over warfare
- *Memoirs Found in a Bathtub* (1961): a Kafkaesque bureaucratic dystopia of endless institutional paranoia
- *His Master's Voice* (1968): scientists attempt to decode a message from space, but the "message" may be a biological technology
- *Cyberiad* (1967): a collection of stories about robots and AI, including the famous "Trurl's Machine"
- **Lem's alien phenomenology: AI and intelligence that we cannot comprehend**
- Quelle: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lem

## 2. Film (1924–1960)

### Aelita (1924) — Jakov Protazanov
- Soviet silent film, based on Alexei Tolstoy's novel
- Mars civilization with expressionist sets, robot workers
- **Earliest international SF film with AI themes**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Britannica, Scaruffi

### Metropolis (1927) — Fritz Lang
- First AI film in history
- Robot Maria: dangerous because she wears a trustworthy human face
- Brigitte Helm as "Maschinenmensch" — one of the first robot representations
- **Set in 2026 — we now live in the future of the film**
- Maria was inducted into the Robot Hall of Fame
- Original props on display at Sony Centre Berlin
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, Robot Hall of Fame, YouTube "Metropolis 1927"

### Der Herr der Welt (1934) — Germany
- "Master of the World" — Überroboter / battle machine
- Worker robots that turn against their masters
- **German SF film, precursor to Metropolis themes**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Loss of Sensation (1935) — USSR
- Soviet film about robots that take over human work
- Workers lose their abilities, robots revolt
- **Early Soviet critique of automation**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Day the Earth Stood Still (1951) — Robert Wise
- Gort — the powerful robot guardian of the alien Klaatu
- "Klaatu barada nikto" — famous command to the robot
- **The robot as guardian angel, not threat**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, The Guardian

### Forbidden Planet (1956) — Fred M. Wilcox
- Robby the Robot: the first "sympathetic" robot
- Safety mechanisms built in — but still dangerous
- **Important: Robby was a star, reused in many films and series (Lost in Space)**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Fly (1958) — Kurt Neumann
- Scientific hubris: a scientist's teleportation experiment goes horribly wrong
- The 1986 Cronenberg remake is a body-horror masterpiece about technology and identity
- **The transporter as trap: when technology dissolves the boundary of self**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Invasion of the Body Snatchers (1956) — Don Siegel
- Based on Jack Finney's novel (1954)
- Alien pods replace humans with identical duplicates — a metaphor for conformity and loss of individuality
- The fear of being replaced by something that looks exactly like you
- **The replacement anxiety: when the copy is indistinguishable from the original**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, The Guardian

### Alphaville (1965) — Jean-Luc Godard
- French New Wave tech-noir film starring Eddie Constantine and Anna Karina
- Alpha 60: a sentient computer that controls the city of Alphaville, banning emotion and poetry
- The computer speaks in a distorted, mechanical voice
- **The computer as totalitarian poet: when the machine controls language itself**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Letterboxd, Movie Crash Course, Facebook

## 3. Television (1950s–1960s)

### The Twilight Zone (1959–1964) — Rod Serling
- Numerous AI and robot episodes: "The Invaders" (1961), "The Lateness of the Hour" (1960), "I Sing the Body Electric" (1962)
- "The Invaders": a woman alone on a farm is terrorized by tiny robots — who turn out to be US astronauts

- "I, Robot" (1964): a robot named Adam is accused of murder; the episode explores robot rights
- "The Demon with a Glass Hand" (1964): a man with a computer hand must solve a mystery
- **The second anthology of AI fear: robots on trial, computers in hands**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, Fandom

## 4. Frühe Computing & Cybernetics (1900–1960)

### Vannevar Bush — "As We May Think" (1945)
- The Atlantic Monthly — visionary essay about computers as human assistants
- Memex — concept for hypertext-based knowledge management
- **The vision that anticipated the Internet and AI**
- Quelle: The Atlantic, Wikipedia

### Alan Turing — "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950)
- Turing Test as a benchmark for machine intelligence
- "Can machines think?" — the fundamental question
- **The philosophical birth of AI**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

### Claude Shannon — "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" (1948)
- Bell System Technical Journal — the foundation of information theory
- Entropy, channel capacity, encoding — the mathematical language of signals
- **The information-theoretic foundation of AI communication**
- Quelle: Bell Labs, Wikipedia, Harvard.edu

### Claude Shannon — Chess-Playing Computer (1950)
- First theoretical design for a chess-playing computer
- Programmed on paper, not executed
- **The vision of the computer as player**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IEEE

### Norbert Wiener — *Cybernetics* (1948)
- "Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine"
- Feedback systems, self-regulation, communication between human and machine
- **The theoretical foundation for AI, robotics, and systems theory**
- Quelle: MIT, History of Information, Britannica

### Macy Conferences (1946–1953)
- Interdisciplinary conferences on cybernetics and feedback systems
- Participants: Wiener, von Neumann, Bateson, Mead, McCulloch
- **The birth hour of interdisciplinarity that gave rise to AI**
- Quelle: JSTOR, Wikipedia, Kate Vass Gallery

### Warren McCulloch & Walter Pitts (1943)
- "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity"
- First mathematical description of an artificial neural network
- **The theoretical birth of neural networks**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, MIT

### John von Neumann & Oskar Morgenstern — *Game Theory* (1944)
- "Theory of Games and Economic Behavior"
- Mathematical foundations for strategic behavior, later central to multi-agent systems
- **The mathematical language of AI interaction**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Princeton

### John von Neumann — Theory of Self-Reproducing Automata (1949–1966)
- Published posthumously in 1966, but worked on since the 1940s
- Cellular automata: machines that can reproduce themselves
- **The theoretical origin of artificial life and self-replication**
- Quelle: University of Illinois Press, Wikipedia, Archive.org

### Ross Ashby — *Design for a Brain* (1952) / *An Introduction to Cybernetics* (1956)
- The Homeostat (1949): first "thinking machine" that self-regulated to equilibrium
- "Requisite Variety" — a system must match the complexity of its environment
- **The first adaptive machine: cybernetics as self-regulation**
- Quelle: Ashby.info, Medium, York.ac.uk

### Grey Walter — *The Living Brain* (1953) / Elmer & Elsie (1948–1949)
- Robotic "tortoises" — Elmer and Elsie (ELectro MEchanical Robots, Light-Sensitive)
- First autonomous robots that could navigate, learn, and self-recognize in mirrors
- **The first biological robots: behavior-based AI before the term existed**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Cybernetic Zoo, ResearchGate, CSULB

### Dartmouth Conference (1956)
- John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, Nathaniel Rochester
- The proposal: "We propose that a 2 month, 10 man study of artificial intelligence be carried out"
- **The term "Artificial Intelligence" was coined here — the official birth of AI**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, LivingInternet, Stanford (jmc.stanford.edu), Council of Science

### Arthur Samuel — Checkers-Playing Program (1959)
- IBM 701, first machine learning application
- The program learned through self-play and improved itself
- **Samuel coined the term "Machine Learning"**
- Quelle: IBM, MIT, ResearchGate

### Logic Theorist (1955–1956) — Newell, Simon & Shaw
- First AI program, proved mathematical theorems
- Demonstrated symbolic reasoning
- **The first proof that computers can "think"**
- Quelle: Carnegie Mellon, AIWS, History of Information

### General Problem Solver (GPS) (1957–1959) — Newell & Simon
- Extension of Logic Theorist, general problem solving
- Means-ends analysis — central algorithm structure
- **The universal problem-solving machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, AIWS, Cow-Shed

### Frank Rosenblatt — Perceptron (1958)
- First artificial neural network with learning capability
- Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory, hardware-based
- **The birth hour of deep learning**
- Quelle: Cornell, Stanford, S-PRO

## 5. Frühe Musik, Elektronik & KI (1900–1960)

### Lejaren Hiller & Leonard Isaacson — Illiac Suite (1957)
- First computer-generated composition on the ILLIAC computer
- University of Illinois, algorithm generated string quartet
- **The first proof: computers can be creative**
- Quelle: CCRMA Stanford, ACM, JSTOR

### Max Mathews — MUSIC (1957)
- Bell Labs, first software for digital music synthesis
- "Daisy Bell" (1961) — the computer sang
- **The foundation of all digital music production**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum

### Alan Turing — First Computer Tones (1951)
- Turing programmed the Mark II computer to generate tones
- Almost forgotten pioneer of computer music
- **AI as creative partner, not just tool**
- Quelle: Wikipedia "Artificial intelligence in music", Medium

### Iannis Xenakis — Algorithmic Composition (1950s–1960s)
- Greek-French composer, architect
- "Metastasis" (1953) — mathematical models for music
- Stochastic music — probability algorithms
- **The bridge between mathematics and art**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer Music Journal

### Daphne Oram — Oramics (1957–1960s)
- British composer and electronic musician (1925–2003)
- Co-founder of the BBC Radiophonic Workshop
- Developed "Oramics": a system of drawing waveforms on 35mm film to create electronic sounds
- **The first drawn sound synthesizer: visual music before the digital era**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, The Guardian, Oramics Machine

### Delia Derbyshire (1937–2001)
- British electronic music pioneer at the BBC Radiophonic Workshop
- Realized the *Doctor Who* theme (1963) using tape manipulation and oscillators
- **The electronic music pioneer: tape as instrument and the birth of the TV theme**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, The Guardian

### Pierre Schaeffer — Musique Concrète (1940s–1950s)
- French composer and engineer (1910–1995)
- Coined "musique concrète" — music constructed from recorded sounds
- *Étude aux chemins de fer* (1948): first piece of musique concrète
- **The first sampler: the world as instrument and the birth of electronic music**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, INA-GRM, The Guardian

### Karlheinz Stockhausen — Electronic Music (1950s–1960s)
- German composer (1928–2007), pioneer of electronic and serial music
- *Gesang der Jünglinge* (1956): first masterpiece of electronic music
- *Kontakte* (1960): electronic music with live performers
- **The electronic music visionary: the studio as instrument**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stockhausen.org, The Guardian

### Morton Subotnick — *Silver Apples of the Moon* (1967)
- American composer (b. 1933), first electronic work commissioned by a record label (Nonesuch)
- Used the Buchla synthesizer — the first modular synthesizer
- **The first electronic album: synthesizer as the new instrument**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Nonesuch Records, The Guardian

### Wendy Carlos — *Switched-On Bach* (1968)
- American composer (b. 1939), pioneer of electronic music
- Transcribed Bach for the Moog synthesizer — the album became a bestseller
- **The synthesizer goes mainstream: classical music reborn as electronic**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Columbia Records, The Guardian

## 6. Frühe Roboter & KI-Experimente

### Shakey the Robot (1966–1972) — SRI International
- The first mobile robot with general reasoning capabilities
- Combined computer vision, natural language, and planning in a physical body
- **The first robot that "thought" while it moved: AI embodied**
- Quelle: SRI International, Wikipedia, AI Magazine

### Stanford Cart (1960s–1970s)
- Remote-controlled cart that navigated across a room in 5 hours (1979)
- Early computer vision: edge detection, path planning, obstacle avoidance
- **The slow birth of autonomous driving: 5 hours to cross a room**
- Quelle: Stanford, Wikipedia, Computer History Museum

## 7. Frühe Spiele

### Bertie the Brain (1950)
- One of the first computers to play Tic-Tac-Toe
- 4 meters tall, vacuum tube-based
- **The first game computer**
- Quelle: Facebook/CTY Johns Hopkins, Wikipedia

### Nimrod (1951)
- Computer that played Nim perfectly
- Edward Condon, Nimatron
- **The machine that defeated humans in games**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Video Games

### Tennis for Two (1958)
- William Higinbotham, Brookhaven National Laboratory
- Analog computer, oscilloscope display
- **Precursor to Pong and all video games**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Museum of Play

### Spacewar! (1962)
- Steve Russell, MIT AI Lab
- First digital video game, two spaceships battle
- **Birth hour of the gaming industry in the AI Lab**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MIT, Museum of Play

### Hamurabi (1964)
- Text-based city management game
- Precursor to SimCity and Civilization
- **The first game simulation of a society**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Early Mainframe Games

### Lunar Lander (1969)
- Simulation of the Moon landing
- Later versions influenced Atari games
- **Simulation as entertainment and education**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Early Mainframe Games

### The Oregon Trail (1971)
- Educational game, simulation of settler life
- Later one of the most influential computer games
- **Simulation as pedagogical tool**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Museum of Play

### Colossal Cave Adventure (1976) / Zork (1980)
- Text adventure games, early AI-like interaction through text parsers
- *Colossal Cave Adventure* (1976): Will Crowther and Don Woods — the first text adventure
- *Zork* (1980): Infocom — the most famous text adventure, inspired by Adventure
- **The first interactive fiction: the player converses with the machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Museum of Play, The Strong
