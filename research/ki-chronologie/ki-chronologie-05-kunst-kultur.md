- The first album to be released as a series of apps — technology as the medium

### Janelle Monáe: *Metropolis* series (2007–2018)
- Concept series following Cindi Mayweather, an android who falls in love with a human
- *Metropolis: Suite I (Chase)* (2007), *The ArchAndroid* (2010), *The Electric Lady* (2013), *Dirty Computer* (2018)
- Themes: android rights, love across the human-machine boundary, totalitarian control
- Monáe's alter ego is Cindi Mayweather — the android as the artist's true self
- **The android soul: Monáe's android as a metaphor for Black and queer identity**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Rolling Stone, Pitchfork, YouTube

### Poppy: *I Disagree* (2020)
- American singer and internet personality who performs as an AI-like persona
- Her YouTube persona started as a satire of internet culture, evolving into a commentary on identity
- The album explores themes of destruction, rebirth, and the dissolution of the self
- **The AI persona: when the pop star is a character, and the character is a machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, YouTube, Pitchfork

### 100 gecs: Hyperpop and AI (2019–present)
- American duo Laura Les and Dylan Brady
- Hyperpop: a genre that uses extreme digital processing, auto-tune, and glitch aesthetics
- The music sounds like it was made by a machine — or by humans imitating machines
- **The hyperpop machine: music that sounds like it was generated by an AI on drugs**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Pitchfork, YouTube

### Auto-Tune (1997) — Antares Audio Technologies
- Pitch correction software invented by Andy Hildebrand, a geophysicist who used seismic data processing
- Initially designed to correct pitch in recordings, but became an aesthetic effect
- T-Pain, Kanye West, Cher: artists who used Auto-Tune as a vocal effect, not just a correction tool
- **The robotic voice: when pitch correction becomes the sound of the machine singing**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Antares, YouTube

### Melodyne — Celemony (2001)
- Advanced pitch and timing editing software that can manipulate individual notes in a recording
- The first software to allow polyphonic pitch correction (editing individual notes in chords)
- Used in nearly all modern music production, making the human voice infinitely editable
- **The voice as putty: when every note can be reshaped by the machine**
- Quelle: Celemony, Wikipedia, YouTube

### LANDR (2014)
- AI-powered online mastering service that uses machine learning to master tracks
- Analyzes the audio and applies EQ, compression, and limiting automatically
- The first AI mastering tool to achieve commercial viability
- **The AI mastering engineer: the machine that polishes the music**
- Quelle: LANDR, Wikipedia, YouTube

### iZotope Ozone — AI-Assisted Mixing (2017)
- Intelligent mixing assistant that analyzes audio and suggests EQ, compression, and reverb settings
- The AI "listens" to the track and makes recommendations based on genre and style
- **The AI mixing assistant: the machine that tells you how your music should sound**
- Quelle: iZotope, Wikipedia, YouTube

### Spleeter by Deezer (2019)
- Open-source AI tool for stem separation (isolating vocals, drums, bass, etc. from a mix)
- Uses deep learning to separate audio sources that were previously inseparable
- The first widely available AI stem separation tool, used by DJs and producers worldwide
- **The audio deconstructor: AI that can unmix a finished song into its parts**
- Quelle: Deezer, GitHub, Wikipedia

### Moises: AI Music Separation (2019–present)
- Mobile and web app that uses AI to separate vocals and instruments from any song
- Allows musicians to practice with isolated tracks, create karaoke versions, and remix songs
- **The practice tool: AI that turns any song into a backing track**
- Quelle: Moises, Wikipedia, YouTube

### Harold Cohen: AARON (1973–2016)
- Computer program that created original abstract art for over 40 years
- Started as line drawings, evolved to color, composition, and eventually physical robots
- **The longest-running AI art project: a machine that painted like a human for decades**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum, Medium (Nettrice Gaskins)

### Karl Sims: Evolved Virtual Creatures (1994)
- Genetic algorithms evolve virtual creatures that walk, swim, and compete
- Artificial evolution in simulated environments: survival of the fittest as art
- **The first evolutionary art: breeding creatures in the computer**
- Quelle: MIT Media Lab, Wikipedia, YouTube

### Scott Draves: Electric Sheep (1999–present)
- Distributed screen-saver that renders "sheep" (fractal animations) via collective computing
- 450,000+ computers participate; users vote on which sheep survive and reproduce
- **The first distributed AI art: millions of humans and machines co-creating**
- Quelle: electricsheep.org, MIT Press, Digital Art Database

### Refik Anadol: Data Sculpture (2016–present)
- Turkish-American artist who creates AI-generated "data sculptures" from archives
- "Unsupervised" at MoMA (2022): AI interpreting the museum's collection in real-time
- **The museum as AI canvas: machine dreams of human art**
- Quelle: MoMA, Wikipedia, Artsy, Hiscox Art and AI Report

### Obvious Art: "Edmond de Belamy" (2018)
- The first AI-generated artwork sold at a major auction house (Christie's, $432,500)
- Created by a Parisian collective using GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks)
- **The auction that made AI art legitimate: the Belamy moment**
- Quelle: Christie's, Wikipedia, CNN, Artsy, Artnome

### Mario Klingemann: Neural Network Art (2017–present)
- German artist who creates "memories of Passersby I" — a GAN that generates portraits in real-time
- Works with latent space, style transfer, and neural network aesthetics
- **The artist as curator of the machine unconscious: exploring latent space**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Artsy, Twitter/X, YouTube

### Sofia Crespo: Generative Biology Art (2018–present)
- Neural networks trained on biological forms to generate imaginary creatures
- "Neural Zoo" and "Artificial Natural History" — the aesthetics of synthetic biology
- **The AI that dreams of nature: generative biology as art practice**
- Quelle: Sofia Crespo website, Ars Electronica, Creative Applications
