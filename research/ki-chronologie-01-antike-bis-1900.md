## 1. Mythische Wurzeln & Frühe Automaten

### Archytas of Tarentum — Flying Wooden Dove (~400 BCE)
- Greek philosopher, mathematician, and strategist from Tarentum (modern Taranto, Italy)
- Constructed a steam-powered wooden dove that could fly, considered the earliest known automaton
- Used aerodynamics and compressed air to create lifelike flight — a mechanical bird that flapped its wings
- **The earliest known automaton: artificial flight before the airplane**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Information, Medium

### Hero of Alexandria — Pneumatic Automata (10–70 CE)
- Greek-Egyptian mathematician and engineer active in Alexandria during the Roman era
- Invented automatic temple doors powered by fire and steam, coin-operated holy water dispensers (the first vending machine)
- Created mechanical singing birds, self-moving figurines, and programmable stage sets in *Pneumatica* and *Automata*
- The aeolipile — a primitive steam engine — precursor to all steam-powered machinery
- **The first systematic study of automation: physics as performance**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Information, Alpha History

### Al-Jazari — The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices (1206)
- Badi' al-Zaman al-Jazari (1136–1206), Muslim polymath from Mesopotamia
- Published *The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices* describing 50 machines
- The elephant clock: water-powered automaton with a mechanical elephant and moving figures
- Musical automaton: a boat with mechanical musicians that played interchangeable cylinders
- Water-raising devices, programmable automata, and early robotics
- **The bridge between ancient and modern automata: engineering as art**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, aljazaribook.com, Met Museum, History Medieval

### Roger Bacon — Brazen Head (13th Century Legend)
- Franciscan friar and philosopher (1214–1292), known as "Doctor Mirabilis"
- Legendary brazen (brass) head that could answer any question — a medieval oracle machine
- The legend inspired later automata and the trope of the talking head
- **The medieval fantasy of an all-knowing machine: the AI oracle before electricity**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Colorado Historic Newspapers, Icy Sedgwick

### Albertus Magnus — Automaton (13th Century Legend)
- Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian (c. 1200–1280)
- Legend of a mechanical servant/android that could answer questions and perform tasks
- Destroyed by his student Thomas Aquinas, who thought it was demonic
- **The fear of the artificial being: even saints feared their own creations**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

### Regiomontanus — Iron Fly (15th Century)
- Johannes Müller von Königsberg (1436–1476), German mathematician and astronomer
- Legend of a mechanical iron fly that could buzz around a room and return to his hand
- Also an iron eagle that could fly to greet the Holy Roman Emperor
- **The Renaissance dream of mechanical flight: artifice as spectacle**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Information

### Leonardo da Vinci — Mechanical Knight (1495)
- Leonardo designed and possibly built a humanoid automaton in 1495
- Based on his anatomical studies — the first design of a humanoid robot
- The knight could sit, stand, raise its visor, and move its arms — all mechanically operated
- Reconstructed by Mark Rosheim in the 20th century based on Leonardo's sketches
- **The first humanoid robot design: anatomy as engineering**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Leonardo's Robot, Endourology PDF, Art Selectronic

## 2. Die Große Automaten-Epoche (17.–19. Jahrhundert)

### Athanasius Kircher — Musurgia Universalis (1650)
- German Jesuit polymath (1602–1680) published *Musurgia Universalis*, a vast encyclopedia of music and acoustics
- Included designs for automatic musical instruments, echo chambers, and speaking tubes
- Described a "cat piano" and other mechanical sound devices
- **The universal science of music: the first systematic study of sound as mechanics**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, McGill University

### Jacques de Vaucanson — Flute Player & Mechanical Duck (1738)
- French inventor (1709–1782) created automata that astonished Europe
- The Flute Player: a life-size automaton that played 12 different tunes on a real flute
- The Digesting Duck: a mechanical duck that appeared to eat, digest, and defecate
- The Duck was a famous philosophical provocation about the nature of life and mechanism
- **The mechanical miracle: the duck that sparked debates about artificial life**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Linda Hall Library, Alpha History, Jessica Riskin (2003)

### Wolfgang von Kempelen — The Mechanical Turk (1770)
- Hungarian inventor and writer Wolfgang von Kempelen created the "Turk" in 1770
- A mechanical chess-playing machine that appeared to play autonomously
- Defeated Napoleon and Benjamin Franklin — toured Europe for decades
- Actually a hoax: a human chess master hid inside and operated the machine via levers
- Edgar Allan Poe exposed the hoax in his 1836 essay "Maelzel's Chess Player"
- **The original AI hoax: the fantasy of mechanical intelligence powered by hidden human labor**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Chess.com, Big Think, Futility Closet

### Henri Maillardet — Draughtsman-Writer Automaton (c. 1800)
- Swiss mechanician Henri Maillardet built the "Draughtsman-Writer" around 1800
- The automaton could write poems in English and French and draw four different images
- It stored "memories" of 13,000 cam-controlled movements — a mechanical program
- Now in the Franklin Institute, Philadelphia — restored in 2007
- **The first mechanical computer memory: a machine that remembered how to draw**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, HowStuffWorks, The Franklin Institute, The Vintage News

## 3. Algorithmische Musik (Pre-1900)

### Johann Philipp Kirnberger — Dice Game (1757)
- German composer and music theorist (1721–1783), student of J.S. Bach
- Published *Der allezeit fertige Polonoisen- und Menuettencomponist* (The Ever-Ready Polonaise and Minuet Composer)
- A dice-based system for generating minuets and polonaises without musical knowledge
- Prefigured algorithmic composition by 200 years
- **The first published algorithmic music system: chance as composer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Amaranth Publishing

### Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart — Musikalisches Würfelspiel (1787)
- Dice-based algorithmic composition system
- 272 pre-composed measures assembled by dice rolls into minuets
- **The first algorithmic composition: chance as composer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Amaranth Publishing, Medium

## 4. Frühe Science Fiction & Literatur (Pre-1900)

### E.T.A. Hoffmann — "Der Sandmann" (1816)
- German Romantic writer (1776–1822) — part of *Nachtstücke* (1816)
- The story of Nathanael, Olympia the automaton, and the sinister Coppelius
- Olympia: a beautiful, life-like mechanical doll that dances and sings — but has no soul
- The story explores the uncanny valley before the term existed: the horror of the almost-human
- Freud later analyzed the tale in his essay "The Uncanny" (1919)
- **The literary birth of the uncanny: the automaton that we desire and fear**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Project Gutenberg, Genealogies of Modernity, Medium

### Mary Shelley — *Frankenstein* (1818)
- British writer (1797–1851), daughter of Mary Wollstonecraft and William Godwin
- Victor Frankenstein creates life from dead matter — the original "mad scientist" narrative
- The creature: intelligent, articulate, and vengeful — not a monster but a rejected child
- **The birth of modern science fiction: the creator who abandons his creation**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Standardliteratur, JSTOR

### Samuel Butler — *Erewhon* (1872)
- British writer (1835–1902) — satirical utopian novel
- The "Book of the Machines": a philosophical treatise within the novel arguing that machines are evolving and may one day supplant humans
- Butler later wrote "Darwin among the Machines" (1863) as a letter to the editor
- **The evolutionary vision of AI: machines as the next stage of evolution**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, McCorduck "Machines Who Think"

### Auguste Villiers de L'Isle-Adam — *L'Ève Future* (1886)
- French symbolist writer (1838–1889)
- Thomas Edison creates an artificial woman (Andréide) for a friend — the perfect, mechanical Eve
- The android is superior to real women: beautiful, obedient, and programmable
- **The misogynistic android: the ideal woman as machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, JSTOR, Standardliteratur

### Kurd Lasswitz — *Auf Zwei Planeten* (1897)
- German philosopher and SF writer (1848–1910), "the father of German science fiction"
- Martians with advanced technology visit Earth — their society is a utopian technocracy
- The Martians use "thinking machines" (Denkmaschinen) for governance and calculation
- **The first German SF novel: technology as the basis of a superior civilization**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, SF Encyclopedia, ResearchGate

### Edgar Allan Poe — "Maelzel's Chess Player" (1836)
- Poe's essay exposed the Mechanical Turk as a fraud using logical deduction
- He observed that the machine made no computational errors — impossible for a true automaton
- Also noted the operator's distractive tactics and the use of concealed mirrors
- **One of the first pieces of AI criticism: the skeptic who saw through the machine**
- Quelle: Edgar Allan Poe Society of Baltimore, ChessBase, Poe Museum

### Nathaniel Hawthorne — "The Birth-Mark" (1843)
- American novelist and short story writer (1804–1864)
- Aylmer, a scientist, attempts to remove a birthmark from his wife Georgiana's face using science
- The story critiques scientific hubris and the pursuit of perfection through technology
- **Early American literary warning: science that destroys what it tries to perfect**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, JSTOR, Study.com

### Fitz-James O'Brien — "The Diamond Lens" (1858)
- Irish-American writer (1828–1862), pioneer of early science fiction
- A scientist invents a microscope so powerful it can see beyond the physical realm
- Discovers a miniature world and a beautiful female creature within a drop of water
- **The fantasy of technology that transcends physical limits: seeing the invisible**
- Quelle: Project Gutenberg, Goodreads, The Himrich Library

### Edward Bulwer-Lytton — *The Coming Race* (1871)
- British novelist and politician (1803–1873)
- A subterranean race called the Vril-ya possess advanced technology and mental powers
- "Vril" — a limitless energy source that becomes a template for later SF energy concepts
- The Vril-ya are androgynous, telepathic, and technologically superior
- **The underground utopia: advanced technology as evolutionary superiority**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Jess Nevins, Skulls in the Stars, Amazon

### Edward Bellamy — *Looking Backward: 2000–1887* (1888)
- American journalist and utopian socialist (1850–1898)
- Julian West wakes up in the year 2000 to find a socialist utopia with industrial armies
- The state controls all industry; citizens retire at 45 with guaranteed income
- Predicted credit cards, shopping malls, and centralized distribution systems
- **The utopian automation dream: technology as the engine of socialist equality**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Behavioral Scientist, EBSCO, Reddit

### William Morris — *News from Nowhere* (1890)
- British textile designer, poet, and socialist (1834–1896)
- Written as a direct response to Bellamy's *Looking Backward*
- Morris rejected Bellamy's industrial, centralized, technological utopia
- Envisioned a bucolic, craft-based, libertarian socialist future with no factories or machines
- **The anti-tech utopia: a deliberate rejection of automation and industrialization**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Marxists Internet Archive, The Elysian, Marshall Colman

### H.G. Wells — Full Works (1866–1946)
- British writer, "the father of science fiction"
- *The Time Machine* (1895): humanity evolves into two species — the Eloi and the Morlocks; technology creates two separate species
- *The War of the Worlds* (1898): Martian invaders with advanced technology defeated by Earth's bacteria, not human technology
- *The First Men in the Moon* (1901): Selenites — insectoid, hive-minded lunar beings with advanced technology
- *The World Set Free* (1914): coined the term "atomic bomb" — prescient about nuclear weapons
- *The Shape of Things to Come* (1933): future history predicting WWII and a world state
- **The prophet of technological futures: from time travel to nuclear destruction**
- Quelle: Standardliteratur, Wikipedia, MindMatters, IJESH, The Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists

### Jules Verne — Selected Works (1828–1905)
- French novelist, pioneer of science fiction
- *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea* (1870): Captain Nemo's Nautilus — a submarine with advanced technology, autonomous from society
- *From the Earth to the Moon* (1865): the Columbiad cannon — technological optimism about space travel
- *The Mysterious Island* (1874): contains a cameo by Captain Nemo and explores technology as survival tool
- *Paris in the Twentieth Century* (1863/1994): predicted fax machines, elevators, and the Internet — but was rejected by his publisher as too unbelievable
- **The technological optimist: science as adventure and progress**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Standardliteratur, Britannica

## 5. Frühe Philosophie des Geistes & KI-Vorläufer

### René Descartes — Mind-Body Dualism (1647)
- "Cogito ergo sum" — der Geist ist vom Körper getrennt
- Tiere und Maschinen als "Automaten"
- **Die philosophische Wurzel des KI-Problems**
- Quelle: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Wikipedia

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan* (1651)
- "Reason is nothing but reckoning" — Denken ist Rechnen
- Mechanistische Theorie der Kognition
- **Die Idee, dass Intelligenz berechenbar ist**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, McCorduck "Machines Who Think"

### Julien Offray de La Mettrie — *L'Homme Machine* (1748)
- Mensch als Maschine, keine Seele, nur Mechanismus
- Radikaler Materialismus
- **Der Mensch als biologische Maschine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, McCorduck

### Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz — Monadology (1714) / Calculating Machine (1673)
- Invented the first mechanical calculator capable of all four basic operations (1673)
- Dreamed of a "characteristica universalis" — a universal symbolic language for reasoning
- **The 17th-century dream of mechanized thought: the computer before electricity**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stephen Wolfram Writings, Cambridge.org

### George Boole — *The Laws of Thought* (1854)
- Boolean Algebra — Logik als Algebra
- Grundlage aller digitalen Computer und KI-Systeme
- **Die mathematische Sprache der KI**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, Kronecker Wallis

### Samuel Butler — "Darwin among the Machines" (1863)
- Maschinen entwickeln sich wie Organismen
- Könnten Bewusstsein entwickeln und Menschen ersetzen
- **Die Evolutionäre Vision der KI**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, McCorduck

## 2. Die literarischen Grundlagen (19. Jahrhundert)

Die literarische Vorstellung künstlicher Intelligenz und autonomer Maschinen entwickelt sich im 19. Jahrhundert aus der Romantik, der Gothic Novel und der frühen Science Fiction. Diese Werke formen die kulturellen Narrative, die bis heute die Debatte um KI prägen.

### Mary Shelley: *Frankenstein* (1818)
- **Autor:** Mary Shelley
- **Jahr:** 1818
- **KI-Relevanz:** Das grundlegende Werk über künstliche Schöpfung und das Verhältnis zwischen Schöpfer und Geschöpf. Victor Frankensteins Monster als erste literarische KI – ein künstliches Wesen, das Bewusstsein, Sprache und Moral entwickelt.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Frankenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankenstein)

### E.T.A. Hoffmann: *Der Sandmann* (1816)
- **Autor:** E.T.A. Hoffmann
- **Jahr:** 1816 (veröffentlicht in *Nachtstücken*)
- **KI-Relevanz:** Die Automatenfigur Olimpia als früheste literarische Darstellung einer humanoiden Maschine, die Menschen täuscht. Grundlegend für das „Uncanny Valley“ und die Frage: Was unterscheidet Mensch von Maschine?
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Der Sandmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sandman_(short_story))

### Edgar Allan Poe: *Maelzel's Chess Player* (1836)
- **Autor:** Edgar Allan Poe
- **Jahr:** 1836
- **KI-Relevanz:** Poe analysiert den „Schachtürken“ – einen scheinbar spielenden Automaten, der in Wahrheit von einem Menschen gesteuert wird. Frühe Auseinandersetzung mit dem Turing-Test-ähnlichen Problem: Wann ist eine Maschine „intelligent“?
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Maelzel's Chess Player](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maelzel%27s_Chess_Player)

### Nathaniel Hawthorne: *The Birth-Mark* (1843)
- **Autor:** Nathaniel Hawthorne
- **Jahr:** 1843
- **KI-Relevanz:** Der Wissenschaftler Aylmer versucht, die Natur durch Technik zu perfektionieren – ein früher technik-kritischer Text über Hybris und die Grenzen menschlicher Kontrolle.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: The Birth-Mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Birth-Mark)

### Fitz-James O'Brien: *The Diamond Lens* (1858)
- **Autor:** Fitz-James O'Brien
- **Jahr:** 1858
- **KI-Relevanz:** Ein Wissenschaftler entwickelt eine mikroskopische Linse und entdeckt eine intelligente Mikrowelt. Frühe Vision von Technologie als Tor zu neuen Formen des Bewusstseins.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Fitz-James O'Brien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitz-James_O%27Brien)

### Edward Bulwer-Lytton: *The Coming Race* (1871)
- **Autor:** Edward Bulwer-Lytton
- **Jahr:** 1871
- **KI-Relevanz:** Die Vril-ya besitzen eine fortschrittliche, fast maschinenartige Zivilisation mit automatischen Geräten und einer „Vril“-Energie, die wie eine universelle KI-ähnliche Kraft wirkt. Beeinflusste spätere utopische und dystopische Science Fiction.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: The Coming Race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Coming_Race)

### Samuel Butler: *Erewhon* (1872)
- **Autor:** Samuel Butler
- **Jahr:** 1872
- **KI-Relevanz:** Der „Buch der Maschinen“-Abschnitt propagiert die Abschaffung von Maschinen aus Furcht vor ihrer zukünftigen Überlegenheit. Früheste literarische KI-Sicherheitsdebatte und Maschinenaufstand-Narrativ.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Erewhon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erewhon)

### Edward Bellamy: *Looking Backward* (1888)
- **Autor:** Edward Bellamy
- **Jahr:** 1888
- **KI-Relevanz:** Die utopische Gesellschaft des Jahres 2000 nutzt automatisierte Produktion und ein zentralisiertes Wirtschaftssystem, das wie ein proto-KI-gesteuertes System funktioniert. Einflussreich für technik-optimistische Zukunftsvisionen.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Looking Backward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Looking_Backward)

### Auguste Villiers de L'Isle-Adam: *L'Ève Future* (1886)
- **Autor:** Auguste Villiers de L'Isle-Adam
- **Jahr:** 1886
- **KI-Relevanz:** Der Erfinder Edison erschafft Hadaly, eine perfekte androide Frau. Eines der frühesten Werke über künstliche Menschen und die Idee, menschliche Perfektion durch Maschinen zu ersetzen. Grundlegend für das „Gynoid“-Konzept.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: L'Ève Future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Future_Eve)

### William Morris: *News from Nowhere* (1890)
- **Autor:** William Morris
- **Jahr:** 1890
- **KI-Relevanz:** Gegenentwurf zu Bellamy – eine dezentralisierte, handwerkliche Utopie, die bewusst auf Automatisierung verzichtet. Wichtig als kritische Stimme in der Debatte um Technologie und Gesellschaft.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: News from Nowhere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/News_from_Nowhere)

### Kurd Lasswitz: *Auf Zwei Planeten* (1897)
- **Autor:** Kurd Lasswitz
- **Jahr:** 1897
- **KI-Relevanz:** Die Marsbewohner besitzen hoch entwickelte Technologie, darunter automatische Maschinen und ein globales Kommunikationsnetz. Lasswitz gilt als „deutscher Jules Verne“ und prägte die deutschsprachige Science Fiction.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Kurd Lasswitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurd_Lasswitz)

### Jules Verne: Werke mit Automaten- und Robotik-Themen
- **Autor:** Jules Verne
- **Relevante Werke:**
  - *Paris im 20. Jahrhundert* (1863, posthum 1994) – automatische Maschinen, Computer, technisierte Welt
  - *Robur der Sieger* (1886) – Die Albatross als autonomes Fluggerät
  - *Die 500 Millionen der Begum* (1879) – automatisierte Stadt Ville-France
- **KI-Relevanz:** Verne beschreibt mechanische Automaten, automatische Städte und Maschinen, die menschliche Arbeit ersetzen. Weniger philosophisch als Wells, aber visionär in der technischen Umsetzung.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: Jules Verne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jules_Verne)

### H.G. Wells: Die frühen Science-Fiction-Klassiker
- **Autor:** H.G. Wells
  - *The Time Machine* (1895) – Eloi und Morlocks als Ergebnis evolutionärer Technik-Abhängigkeit
  - *The War of the Worlds* (1898) – Außerirdische Technologie als überlegene Intelligenz
  - *The First Men in the Moon* (1901) – Die Seleniten als technisch-organisierte Zivilisation
  - *The World Set Free* (1914) – „Atomic bombs“ und technologische Lösungen globaler Probleme
  - *The Shape of Things to Come* (1933) – Weltregierung durch technokratische Elite und „Wings Over the World“
- **KI-Relevanz:** Wells entwickelt das Narrativ der technologischen Singularität, der außerirdischen Überlegenheit und der technokratischen Weltordnung. Seine Werke sind Brücke zwischen 19. Jahrhundert und moderner KI-Dystopie.
- **Quelle:** [Wikipedia: H.G. Wells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H._G._Wells)

### Weitere bedeutende Werke des 19. Jahrhunderts

**Hoffmanns weitere Automaten-Erzählungen:**
- *Die Automate* (1814) – Figurenspiel-Automaten und Illusion
- *Die Doppeltgängerin* – Verwechslung von Mensch und Maschine

**Frühe Utopien und Dystopien:**
- *The Steam Man of the Prairies* (1868) von Edward S. Ellis – erster „Steam Man" in amerikanischer Literatur
- *The Frank Reade Library* (1882-1898) – Serienromane über mechanische Männer und automatische Fortbewegung
- *Auf der Venus* (1895) von Robert Kraft – deutsche Science Fiction mit Automaten-Themen

### Zusammenfassung

Das 19. Jahrhundert etabliert die literarischen Grundmuster der KI-Debatte:
1. **Kreation und Hybris** (Frankenstein, The Birth-Mark)
2. **Automat und Mensch** (Der Sandmann, Villiers' Hadaly)
3. **Maschinenaufstand** (Erewhon, Wells' Morlocks)
4. **Technische Utopie vs. Dystopie** (Bellamy vs. Morris, Wells)
5. **Die perfekte künstliche Frau** (L'Ève Future)
6. **Automatische Zivilisation** (Verne, Lasswitz)

Diese Narrative prägen bis heute die kulturelle Wahrnehmung von KI – von der Angst vor dem „Frankenstein-Moment“ bis zur Hoffnung auf technische Erlösung.
