## 1. KI in der Kunst (Cross-Era)

### Harold Cohen — AARON (1973–2016)
- Computer program that created original abstract art for over 40 years
- Started as line drawings, evolved to color, composition, and eventually physical robots
- **The longest-running AI art project: a machine that painted like a human for decades**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum, Medium (Nettrice Gaskins)

### Karl Sims — Evolved Virtual Creatures (1994)
- Genetic algorithms evolve virtual creatures that walk, swim, and compete
- Artificial evolution in simulated environments: survival of the fittest as art
- **The first evolutionary art: breeding creatures in the computer**
- Quelle: MIT Media Lab, Wikipedia, YouTube

### Scott Draves — Electric Sheep (1999–present)
- Distributed screen-saver that renders "sheep" (fractal animations) via collective computing
- 450,000+ computers participate; users vote on which sheep survive and reproduce
- **The first distributed AI art: millions of humans and machines co-creating**
- Quelle: electricsheep.org, MIT Press, Digital Art Database

### Refik Anadol — Data Sculpture (2016–present)
- Turkish-American artist who creates AI-generated "data sculptures" from archives
- "Unsupervised" at MoMA (2022): AI interpreting the museum's collection in real-time
- **The museum as AI canvas: machine dreams of human art**
- Quelle: MoMA, Wikipedia, Artsy, Hiscox Art and AI Report

### Obvious Art — "Edmond de Belamy" (2018)
- The first AI-generated artwork sold at a major auction house (Christie's, $432,500)
- Created by a Parisian collective using GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks)
- **The auction that made AI art legitimate: the Belamy moment**
- Quelle: Christie's, Wikipedia, CNN, Artsy, Artnome

### Mario Klingemann — Neural Network Art (2017–present)
- German artist who creates "memories of Passersby I" — a GAN that generates portraits in real-time
- Works with latent space, style transfer, and neural network aesthetics
- **The artist as curator of the machine unconscious: exploring latent space**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Artsy, Twitter/X, YouTube

### Sofia Crespo — Generative Biology Art (2018–present)
- Neural networks trained on biological forms to generate imaginary creatures
- "Neural Zoo" and "Artificial Natural History" — the aesthetics of synthetic biology
- **The AI that dreams of nature: generative biology as art practice**
- Quelle: Sofia Crespo website, Ars Electronica, Creative Applications

---

### Frühe Computer-Kunst-Pioniere (1960s–1970s)
- **Manfred Mohr**: algorithmic art using computers since 1968, known for "Cubic Limit" series
- **Vera Molnár**: Hungarian-French artist, pioneer of computer art since 1968, "Des Ordres" (1974)
- **Frieder Nake**: German mathematician and artist, "Matrix Multiplication" (1967), one of the first computer-generated drawings
- **Georg Nees**: German computer art pioneer, "Schotter" (1968) — randomness and order in algorithmic art
- **A. Michael Noll**: Bell Labs researcher, created computer-generated patterns and "Computer Composition with Lines" (1965), inspired by Mondrian
- **Edward Zajec**: Slovenian artist, "The First Visualization" (1968) — early computer animation
- **Roman Verostko**: American artist, "Epigenetic Painting" — algorithmic art that mimics natural growth
- **The first computer artists: when the algorithm became the brush**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum, ZKM, Victoria and Albert Museum

## 2. KI in der Musik (Cross-Era)

### Algorithmische & Generative Musik (Pre-Digital)
- **Athanasius Kircher — Musurgia Universalis (1650)**: encyclopedia of music mechanics, automatic instruments, speaking tubes
- **Johann Philipp Kirnberger — Dice Game (1757)**: dice-based composition system for minuets and polonaises
- **Mozart — Musikalisches Würfelspiel (1787)**: 272 measures assembled by dice rolls into minuets
- **The pre-digital algorithms: when chance was the composer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Amaranth Publishing, Medium, Stanford, McGill University

---

### Frühe Computer-Musik (1950s–1960s)
- **Lejaren Hiller & Leonard Isaacson — Illiac Suite (1957)**: first computer-generated composition on the ILLIAC computer
- **Max Mathews — MUSIC (1957)**: Bell Labs, first software for digital music synthesis; "Daisy Bell" (1961)
- **Alan Turing — First Computer Tones (1951)**: Mark II computer generating tones, forgotten pioneer
- **Iannis Xenakis — Algorithmic Composition (1950s–1960s)**: "Metastasis" (1953), stochastic music, probability algorithms
- **The first computer music: the machine that learned to sing**
- Quelle: CCRMA Stanford, ACM, JSTOR, Computer History Museum, Wikipedia

---

### Elektronische Musik-Pioniere (1940s–1970s)
- **Daphne Oram — Oramics (1957–1960s)**: drawn sound synthesizer, co-founder of BBC Radiophonic Workshop
- **Delia Derbyshire (1937–2001)**: BBC Radiophonic Workshop, *Doctor Who* theme (1963) via tape manipulation
- **Pierre Schaeffer — Musique Concrète (1940s–1950s)**: *Étude aux chemins de fer* (1948), first musique concrète piece
- **Karlheinz Stockhausen (1950s–1960s)**: *Gesang der Jünglinge* (1956), *Kontakte* (1960), electronic music pioneer
- **Morton Subotnick — Silver Apples of the Moon (1967)**: first electronic work commissioned by a record label (Nonesuch), Buchla synthesizer
- **Wendy Carlos — Switched-On Bach (1968)**: Bach transcribed for Moog synthesizer, bestselling electronic album
- **The electronic pioneers: the studio as the new instrument**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, The Guardian, Oramics Machine, Stockhausen.org, Nonesuch Records

---

### Generative & Algorithmische Musik (1970s–1990s)
- **Brian Eno — Generative Music (1970s–present)**: "Ambient 1: Music for Airports" (1979), systems that create music
- **Kraftwerk (1970s–1980s)**: "Die Roboter" (1978), "Computer World" (1981), humans as machines, machines as humans
- **David Cope — EMI (1980s–1990s)**: Experiments in Musical Intelligence, composes in the style of Bach, Mozart, Beethoven
- **Emily Howell (2000s)**: successor to EMI, independent "composer" that creates music autonomously
- **Intelligent Dance Music (IDM) — 1990s**: Aphex Twin, Autechre, μ-Ziq — "intelligent" association with AI
- **Detroit Techno — 1980s–1990s**: Juan Atkins, Derrick May, Kevin Saunderson, futuristic machine rhythms
- **The generative and algorithmic wave: the system as the composer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Ars Electronica, CodeWeek.eu, Sounding Future, Computer History Museum, Prototypr

---

### KI-Musik (2000–2026)
- **AIVA (2016)**: Artificial Intelligence Virtual Artist, classical and soundtrack composition in 250+ styles
- **Amper Music (2016–2020)**: AI music composition for content creators, acquired by Shutterstock
- **Jukedeck (2012–2019)**: British AI music startup, acquired by TikTok/ByteDance
- **Holly Herndon — PROTO (2019)**: album created with "Spawn," AI trained on Herndon's voice, credited creative partner
- **Grimes — Miss Anthropocene (2020)**: concept album about AI as gods, "We Appreciate Power"
- **Flow Machines / Sony CSL — "Daddy's Car" (2016)**: AI-generated song in the style of The Beatles
- **Taryn Southern — "Break Free" (2017) / I AM AI (2018)**: first pop album composed with AI, chart success
- **Auto-Tune / Melodyne (1997–present)**: pitch correction as creative effect, the "robot voice" aesthetic
- **LANDR (2014)**: AI-powered mastering, democratization of the final production step
- **Spleeter (2019)**: AI audio source separation, splits songs into stems
- **Suno (2023) / Udio (2024)**: text-to-music generation, full songs from text prompts
- **Boomy (2019)**: AI music generation and monetization platform
- **Jukebox (2020)**: OpenAI's music generation model, raw audio in multiple genres
- **Magenta (2016)**: Google's TensorFlow project for AI music and art, NSynth, MusicVAE
- **The AI music revolution: the prompt as the new composition**
- Quelle: AIVA, Wikipedia, The Fader, Barbican, Pitchfork, Sony CSL, TarynSouthern.com, BBC, OpenAI, Google Magenta, TechCrunch, The Verge

---

### Pop, Rock & Elektronische Musik mit KI-Themen (1970s–2020s)
- **Alan Parsons Project — I Robot (1977)**: concept album inspired by Asimov's robot stories
- **Styx — "Mr. Roboto" (1983)**: rock opera about robots enforcing music bans, "domo arigato"
- **Gary Numan (1979)**: synth-pop pioneer, "Cars" (1979), "Are 'Friends' Electric?" (1979), android persona
- **Devo (1980)**: "Whip It" (1980), de-evolution concept, red energy dome hats
- **Daft Punk (1997–2021)**: French electronic duo, robot personas, *Interstella 5555* (2003)
- **Radiohead — OK Computer (1997) / Kid A (2000)**: "Paranoid Android" (1997), "Fitter Happier" (1997), text-to-speech
- **Björk (1997–2001)**: "All Is Full of Love" (1999) — robots embracing in the video
- **Janelle Monáe — Metropolis (2007–present)**: Afrofuturist concept series, Cindi Mayweather android
- **Poppy (2020–2021)**: internet pop star turned metal artist, robotic persona, "I Disagree"
- **100 gecs (2019–2023)**: hyperpop duo, algorithmic-sounding maximalist production
- **The pop music of the machine: the android as the pop star**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Arista Records, A&M Records, Beggars Banquet, Virgin Records, Parlophone, Bad Boy Records, Sumerian Records, AllMusic

## 3. KI in der Architektur (Cross-Era)

### Radikale & Responsive Architektur (1960s–1970s)
- **Archigram (1960s)**: "Walking City" (1964), "Plug-In City" (1964), "Instant City" (1968)
- **Cedric Price — Fun Palace (1961–1964)**: "socially interactive machine," cranes, escalators, movable walls
- **Constant Nieuwenhuys — New Babylon (1959–1974)**: post-capitalist city, automated labor, Situationist wandering
- **Superstudio (1966–1978)**: "The Continuous Monument" (1969), "Supersurface" (1972), grid as totalizing system
- **Buckminster Fuller (1960s–1970s)**: geodesic domes, Dymaxion concept, optimal machine for living
- **Yona Friedman — Ville Spatiale (1958–ongoing)**: mobile participatory architecture, framework for human adaptation
- **Nicholas Negroponte — The Architecture Machine (1970)**: MIT Media Lab, AI as creative partner in architecture
- **The radical architecture of the 60s: the building as a machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Monoskop, Archigram Archives, ResearchGate, Academia.edu, Stedelijk Museum, Constant Foundation, MAXXI, MoMA, Buckminster Fuller Institute, Harvard GSD, MIT Press

---

### Parametrische & Algorithmische Architektur (2000s–present)
- **Zaha Hadid Architects — Parametric Design (2000s–present)**: fluid organic forms, Patrik Schumacher's "Parametricism" (2008)
- **Rem Koolhaas / OMA (2000s–present)**: CCTV Headquarters (2012), Prada stores, architecture of data flows
- **MVRDV (1990s–present)**: Market Hall Rotterdam (2014), data-driven computational design
- **BIG / Bjarke Ingels Group (2005–present)**: Copenhill (2019), parametric and sustainable design
- **Neri Oxman — Material Ecology (2010s–present)**: MIT Media Lab, "Silk Pavilion" (2013), biology + algorithms
- **Achim Menges — ICD/ITKE (2010s–present)**: University of Stuttgart, robotic fabrication, biomimetic algorithms
- **Gramazio Kohler Research — ETH Zurich (2005–present)**: "Robotic Wall" (2010), robotic bricklaying
- **Autodesk Generative Design (2016–present)**: AI-generated design options, used by GM, Airbus, Under Armour
- **Spacemaker (2016–2020)**: AI urban planning, acquired by Autodesk for $240 million
- **The parametric and algorithmic wave: the building as a calculation**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, OMA, MVRDV, BIG, MIT Media Lab, ICD Stuttgart, ETH Zurich, Autodesk, Dezeen, ArchDaily, MoMA, The Guardian

---

### Smart Cities & KI-Urbanismus (2000s–present)
- **Sidewalk Labs (2015–2020)**: Google's smart city project, Quayside Toronto, canceled due to privacy concerns
- **Masdar City (2008–present)**: Abu Dhabi, carbon-neutral planned city, AI-managed energy and transport
- **Songdo IBD (2009–present)**: South Korea, city built from scratch with sensors everywhere, AI-managed infrastructure
- **NEOM / The Line (2017–present)**: Saudi Arabia, 170km linear city, AI-managed, no cars, no streets
- **Toyota Woven City (2020–present)**: Japan, prototype city at Mount Fuji, testbed for autonomous vehicles and robotics
- **The smart city dream: the city as a computer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Sidewalk Labs, Masdar City, Songdo IBD, NEOM, Toyota, The Guardian, Dezeen, The Verge, TechCrunch

## 4. Comics & Manga (Cross-Era)

### Frühe Comics & Manga (1950s–1980s)
- **Astro Boy (1952) — Osamu Tezuka**: the original robot boy, robot rights, discrimination
- **Gigantor / Tetsujin 28-go (1956) — Mitsuteru Yokoyama**: giant remote-controlled robot, first mecha anime
- **Doraemon (1969) — Fujiko F. Fujio**: robot cat from the future, friendly helper
- **8th Man (1963) — Kazumasa Hirai**: cyborg detective, early cyberpunk anime
- **Akira (1982–1990 Manga) — Katsuhiro Otomo**: Neo-Tokyo, telepathic children, biological experiments
- **Ghost in the Shell (1989 Manga) — Masamune Shirow**: cyberpunk classic, Major Kusanagi questions identity
- **Appleseed (1985–1989) — Masamune Shirow**: bioroids managing city systems, ethics of artificial governance
- **The foundational manga: the robot child and the cyborg detective**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Anime News Network, New York Times, MDPI, Dark Horse Comics

---

### Klassische & Einflussreiche Manga/Comics (1990s–2000s)
- **Battle Angel Alita / Gunnm (1990) — Yukito Kishiro**: cyborg girl in post-apocalyptic scrapyard, influential cyberpunk
- **Pluto (2003–2009) — Naoki Urasawa**: reimagining of Astro Boy, robot detective Gesicht investigates murders
- **Planetes (1999–2004) — Makoto Yukimura**: space debris collectors, AI systems managing spacecraft
- **2000 AD (1977–present)**: British comic anthology, Judge Dredd, dystopian future with robots
- **Transmetropolitan (1997–2002) — Warren Ellis, Darick Robertson**: dystopian 23rd century, AI assistants, uploaded consciousnesses
- **The classic manga and comics: the robot as the hero and the victim**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Anime News Network, Paste Magazine, CBR, Kodansha, 2000 AD, Vertigo/DC Comics, Goodreads

---

- **Descender (2015–2018) — Jeff Lemire, Dustin Nguyen**: robot boy TIM-21 in a universe where robots were destroyed
- **Saga (2012–present) — Brian K. Vaughan, Fiona Staples**: Robot Kingdom with TV-headed Prince Robot IV, robot culture and politics
- **Monstress (2015–present) — Marjorie Liu, Sana Takeda**: steampunk fantasy, AI-like Old Gods as divine power
- **Big Hero 6 (2014) — Disney/Marvel**: Baymax — caring robot who becomes superhero, "I am satisfied with my care"
- **The Vision (2015–2016) — Tom King, Gabriel Walta**: android creates synthetic family in suburbs, domestic tragedy
- **Tokyo Ghost (2015–2016) — Rick Remender, Sean Murphy**: 2089 VR addiction dystopia, humanity plugged in
- **A.D.: After Death (2016–2017) — Scott Snyder, Jeff Lemire**: post-death world, technology cures final human limitation
- **Machine Man (1978, 2020) — Marvel Comics**: Aaron Stack, robot who upgrades himself to become more human
- **Y: The Last Man (2002–2008) — Brian K. Vaughan, Pia Guerra**: last man and monkey, AI as hope for species survival
- **Ex Machina (2004–2010) — Brian K. Vaughan, Tony Harris**: superhero mayor who can talk to machines (NOT the 2014 film)
- **Doom Patrol — Robotman, Cyborg, Negative Man (DC Comics)**: human brain in robot body, human-machine hybrids
- **Swamp Thing — The Floronic Man, The Green (DC Comics)**: planetary consciousness, organic internet
- **The contemporary comics: the robot as family, as god, as victim**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Image Comics, Marvel Comics, DC Comics, Goodreads, Fandom, The Guardian

## 5. Spiele (Cross-Era)

### Frühe Spiele (1950s–1970s)
- **Bertie the Brain (1950)**: first Tic-Tac-Toe computer, 4 meters tall, vacuum tubes
- **Nimrod (1951)**: computer that played Nim perfectly
- **Tennis for Two (1958)**: analog computer, oscilloscope display, precursor to Pong
- **Spacewar! (1962)**: first digital video game, MIT AI Lab, two spaceships battle
- **Hamurabi (1964)**: text-based city management, precursor to SimCity and Civilization
- **Lunar Lander (1969)**: Moon landing simulation
- **The Oregon Trail (1971)**: educational simulation of settler life
- **Colossal Cave Adventure (1976)**: first text adventure, Will Crowther and Don Woods
- **Zork (1980)**: Infocom's most famous text adventure
- **The first games: the machine as the game master**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Museum of Play, The Strong, Early Mainframe Games, MIT, Infocom

---

### 8-Bit & 16-Bit Era (1980s–1990s)
- **Elite (1984) — David Braben & Ian Bell**: procedural generation of 8 galaxies in 22KB, birth of procedural generation
- **Mega Man (1987) — Capcom**: robot fighting other robots, early video game about AI and robotics
- **SimCity (1989) — Will Wright / Maxis**: city-building simulation, algorithm as mayor
- **The Hobbit (1982)**: text adventure with advanced parser, early natural language interaction in games
- **The 8-bit era: the infinite worlds from finite code**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Ian Bell's Elite Pages, Maxis, Capcom, Museum of Play

---

### Simulation & Strategy (1990s–2000s)
- **Civilization (1991) — Sid Meier / MicroProse**: turn-based strategy, AI opponents with distinct personalities
- **Master of Orion (1993) — SimTex**: 4X space strategy, AI with racial traits and strategies
- **Creatures (1996) — Creature Labs**: artificial life with digital DNA, creatures that learn and evolve
- **StarCraft (1998) — Blizzard**: real-time strategy, Zerg swarm AI as hive mind
- **Total Annihilation (1997) — Cavedog**: thousands of AI-controlled units, tactical formations
- **Baldur's Gate (1998) — BioWare**: AI party members, tactical combat, Infinity Engine
- **System Shock (1994) — Looking Glass**: SHODAN, iconic rogue AI villain
- **System Shock 2 (1999) — Irrational Games**: The Many, biological hive mind controlled by SHODAN
- **The simulation era: the AI as the opponent, the companion, the villain**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MicroProse, SimTex, Blizzard, Cavedog, BioWare, Looking Glass, Irrational Games, Nightdive Studios

---

### KI-Intensive Spiele (2000s–2020s)
- **The Sims (2000) — Maxis**: life simulation, AI-driven needs and social behavior
- **Black & White (2001) — Lionhead**: creature learns from player's morality, AI as ethical mirror
- **Spore (2008) — Maxis / Will Wright**: evolution simulation, procedural generation of creatures and civilizations
- **Fable (2004) — Lionhead**: moral choice system, AI world that remembers and judges
- **Elder Scrolls IV: Oblivion (2006) — Bethesda**: "Radiant AI" — NPCs with schedules, needs, emergent behavior
- **Dwarf Fortress (2006) — Tarn Adams**: most complex simulation, dwarves with personalities and mental health
- **RimWorld (2013) — Tynan Sylvester**: "AI Storyteller" — Cassandra, Phoebe, Randy create narrative events
- **Stellaris (2016) — Paradox**: AI empires with ethics, diplomacy, and endgame "Crisis" factions
- **Detroit: Become Human (2018) — Quantic Dream**: three androids navigate revolution, player choices determine AI equality
- **Observation (2019) — No Code**: you play as SAM the AI, surveillance as gameplay
- **Soma (2015) — Frictional Games**: underwater horror about consciousness, WAU AI preserves humans by any means
- **Portal (2007) / Portal 2 (2011) — Valve**: GLaDOS, the sarcastic AI that tests (and tries to kill) the player
- **The Talos Principle (2014) — Croteam**: philosophical puzzle game about AI consciousness and free will
- **BioShock (2007) — Irrational Games**: Rapture's AI and automated systems, the city as a failed experiment
- **Deus Ex (2000) — Ion Storm**: conspiracy-filled RPG, AI Helios offers to merge with the player
- **KOTOR (2003) — BioWare**: HK-47, the assassin droid with a dark sense of humor
- **Mass Effect (2007) — BioWare**: Geth, EDI, the Reapers — AI civilizations and machine gods
- **Dragon Age (2009) — BioWare**: golems, dwarven automata, the ethics of creating life
- **Nier: Automata (2017) — PlatinumGames**: androids fighting for humanity, existential themes, multiple endings
- **Horizon Zero Dawn (2017) — Guerrilla Games**: post-apocalyptic world where robotic animals roam, AI GAIA managed the ecosystem
- **Subnautica (2018) — Unknown Worlds**: AI PDA guides the player through an alien ocean, survival with AI assistance
- **Stray (2022) — BlueTwelve**: a cat navigates a world of robots, AI companions in a post-human city
- **Atomic Heart (2023) — Mundfish**: Soviet retrofuturistic FPS, robots gone rogue in an alternate 1955
- **The AI game era: the machine as the storyteller, the villain, the companion, the world**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Maxis, Lionhead, Bethesda, Quantic Dream, No Code, Frictional Games, Valve, Croteam, Irrational Games, Ion Storm, BioWare, PlatinumGames, Guerrilla Games, Unknown Worlds, BlueTwelve, Mundfish, TechCrunch, Game Developer, Ludeon Studios, Paradox Interactive
