## 1. Mythische Wurzeln (Antike–Mittelalter)

### Archytas of Tarentum — Flying Wooden Dove (~400 BCE)
- Greek philosopher, mathematician, and strategist from Tarentum (modern Taranto, Italy)
- Constructed a steam-powered wooden dove that could fly, considered the earliest known automaton
- Used aerodynamics and compressed air to create lifelike flight — a mechanical bird that flapped its wings
- **The earliest known automaton: artificial flight before the airplane**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Information, Medium

### Hero of Alexandria — Pneumatic Automata (10–70 CE)
- Greek-Egyptian mathematician and engineer active in Alexandria during the Roman era
- Invented automatic temple doors powered by fire and steam, coin-operated holy water dispensers (the first vending machine)
- Created mechanical singing birds, self-moving figurines, and programmable stage sets in *Pneumatica* and *Automata*
- The aeolipile — a primitive steam engine — precursor to all steam-powered machinery
- **The first systematic study of automation: physics as performance**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Information, Alpha History

### Al-Jazari — The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices (1206)
- Badi' al-Zaman al-Jazari (1136–1206), Muslim polymath from Mesopotamia
- Published *The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices* describing 50 machines
- The elephant clock: water-powered automaton with a mechanical elephant and moving figures
- Musical automaton: a boat with mechanical musicians that played interchangeable cylinders
- Water-raising devices, programmable automata, and early robotics
- **The bridge between ancient and modern automata: engineering as art**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, aljazaribook.com, Met Museum, History Medieval

### Roger Bacon — Brazen Head (13th Century Legend)
- Franciscan friar and philosopher (1214–1292), known as "Doctor Mirabilis"
- Legendary brazen (brass) head that could answer any question — a medieval oracle machine
- The legend inspired later automata and the trope of the talking head
- **The medieval fantasy of an all-knowing machine: the AI oracle before electricity**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Colorado Historic Newspapers, Icy Sedgwick

### Albertus Magnus — Automaton (13th Century Legend)
- Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian (c. 1200–1280)
- Legend of a mechanical servant/android that could answer questions and perform tasks
- Destroyed by his student Thomas Aquinas, who thought it was demonic
- **The fear of the artificial being: even saints feared their own creations**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

### Regiomontanus — Iron Fly (15th Century)
- Johannes Müller von Königsberg (1436–1476), German mathematician and astronomer
- Legend of a mechanical iron fly that could buzz around a room and return to his hand
- Also an iron eagle that could fly to greet the Holy Roman Emperor
- **The Renaissance dream of mechanical flight: artifice as spectacle**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, History of Information

### Leonardo da Vinci — Mechanical Knight (1495)
- Leonardo designed and possibly built a humanoid automaton in 1495
- Based on his anatomical studies — the first design of a humanoid robot
- The knight could sit, stand, raise its visor, and move its arms — all mechanically operated
- Reconstructed by Mark Rosheim in the 20th century based on Leonardo's sketches
- **The first humanoid robot design: anatomy as engineering**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Leonardo's Robot, Endourology PDF, Art Selectronic

### Wolfgang von Kempelen — The Mechanical Turk (1770)
- Hungarian inventor and writer Wolfgang von Kempelen created the "Turk" in 1770
- A mechanical chess-playing machine that appeared to play autonomously
- Defeated Napoleon and Benjamin Franklin — toured Europe for decades
- Actually a hoax: a human chess master hid inside and operated the machine via levers
- Edgar Allan Poe exposed the hoax in his 1836 essay "Maelzel's Chess Player"
- **The original AI hoax: the fantasy of mechanical intelligence powered by hidden human labor**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Chess.com, Big Think, Futility Closet

### Henri Maillardet — Draughtsman-Writer Automaton (c. 1800)
- Swiss mechanician Henri Maillardet built the "Draughtsman-Writer" around 1800
- The automaton could write poems in English and French and draw four different images
- It stored "memories" of 13,000 cam-controlled movements — a mechanical program
- Now in the Franklin Institute, Philadelphia — restored in 2007
- **The first mechanical computer memory: a machine that remembered how to draw**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, HowStuffWorks, The Franklin Institute, The Vintage News

### Edgar Allan Poe — "Maelzel's Chess Player" (1836)
- Poe's essay exposed the Mechanical Turk as a fraud using logical deduction
- He observed that the machine made no computational errors — impossible for a true automaton
- Also noted the operator's distractive tactics and the use of concealed mirrors
- **One of the first pieces of AI criticism: the skeptic who saw through the machine**
- Quelle: Edgar Allan Poe Society of Baltimore, ChessBase, Poe Museum

### Nathaniel Hawthorne: "The Birth-Mark" (1843)
- American novelist and short story writer (1804–1864)
- Aylmer, a scientist, attempts to remove a birthmark from his wife Georgiana's face using science
- The story critiques scientific hubris and the pursuit of perfection through technology
- **Early American literary warning: science that destroys what it tries to perfect**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, JSTOR, Study.com

### Fitz-James O'Brien: "The Diamond Lens" (1858)
- Irish-American writer (1828–1862), pioneer of early science fiction
- A scientist invents a microscope so powerful it can see beyond the physical realm
- Discovers a miniature world and a beautiful female creature within a drop of water
- **The fantasy of technology that transcends physical limits: seeing the invisible**
- Quelle: Project Gutenberg, Goodreads, The Himrich Library

### Edward Bulwer-Lytton: *The Coming Race* (1871)
- British novelist and politician (1803–1873)
- A subterranean race called the Vril-ya possess advanced technology and mental powers
- "Vril" — a limitless energy source that becomes a template for later SF energy concepts
- The Vril-ya are androgynous, telepathic, and technologically superior
- **The underground utopia: advanced technology as evolutionary superiority**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Jess Nevins, Skulls in the Stars, Amazon

### Edward Bellamy: *Looking Backward: 2000–1887* (1888)
- American journalist and utopian socialist (1850–1898)
- Julian West wakes up in the year 2000 to find a socialist utopia with industrial armies
- The state controls all industry; citizens retire at 45 with guaranteed income
- Predicted credit cards, shopping malls, and centralized distribution systems
- **The utopian automation dream: technology as the engine of socialist equality**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Behavioral Scientist, EBSCO, Reddit

### William Morris: *News from Nowhere* (1890)
- British textile designer, poet, and socialist (1834–1896)
- Written as a direct response to Bellamy's *Looking Backward*
- Morris rejected Bellamy's industrial, centralized, technological utopia
- Envisioned a bucolic, craft-based, libertarian socialist future with no factories or machines
- **The anti-tech utopia: a deliberate rejection of automation and industrialization**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Marxists Internet Archive, The Elysian, Marshall Colman

### H.G. Wells: *The Time Machine* (1895)
- British writer (1866–1946), "the father of science fiction"
- The Time Traveller journeys to 802,701 AD and finds humanity evolved into two species
- The Eloi: docile, beautiful, childlike surface dwellers; the Morlocks: subterranean, brutish, industrial
- **The dark side of class division: technology creates two separate species**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MindMatters, IJESH, Quora

### H.G. Wells: *The War of the Worlds* (1898)
- Martian invaders with advanced technology — heat-rays, poison gas, tripod fighting machines
- The Martians are ultimately defeated by Earth's bacteria, not human technology
- **The humbling of technology: advanced alien tech defeated by natural biology**
- Quelle: Standardliteratur, Wikipedia

### H.G. Wells: *The First Men in the Moon* (1901)
- Selenites: insectoid, hive-minded lunar beings with advanced technology
- An insectoid society that mirrors fears of dehumanization through technology
- **The insectoid alien: technology as the evolution of the hive mind**
- Quelle: Standardliteratur, Wikipedia

### H.G. Wells: *The World Set Free* (1914)
- Wells coined the term "atomic bomb" in this novel, predicting nuclear weapons
- Describes atomic energy as both utopian power source and apocalyptic weapon
- Written just before WWI — prescient about the destructive potential of science
- **The prophet of nuclear destruction: predicting atomic warfare before the atom was split**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, The Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, Reddit, QI/Official Facebook

### H.G. Wells: *The Shape of Things to Come* (1933)
- Future history spanning 1933 to 2106, predicting a world state after decades of war
- Predicted WWII, aerial bombardment, and the emergence of a global technocratic government
- The "Air Dictatorship" — a benevolent world government maintained by technological superiority
- **The ultimate prediction novel: a world shaped by technology and war**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Goodreads, Amazon, Reddit

### Kir Bulychev: Alice, A Girl from the Earth (1965–2003)

### Strugatsky Brothers: Monday Begins on Saturday (1965)

### Strugatsky Brothers: Hard to Be a God (1964)

### Gaston Leroux: *The Phantom of the Opera* (1910)
- French journalist and novelist (1868–1927)
- The Phantom's lair beneath the Paris Opera contains elaborate mechanical devices and automata

- **The Gothic automaton: the mechanical as horror and seduction**

### Hugo Gernsback: *Ralph 124C 41+* (1911)
- Luxembourg-American inventor and publisher (1884–1967), coined the term "science fiction"
- Serialized 1911–1912, published as novel in 1925
- Predicted television, radar, solar energy, spaceflight, and remote control
- The protagonist is a brilliant scientist in a utopian future of 2660
- **The technologist's utopia: early SF as a catalogue of future inventions**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, EBSCO, The Periodic Fable, UMN Press

### Karel Čapek: *R.U.R.* (1920) — *The Makropulos Affair* (1922) — *Krakatit* (1924)
- Čapek (1890–1938), Czech playwright and novelist, coined the word "robot"
- *R.U.R.*: Rossum's Universal Robots — the birth of the word "robot" (Czech: *robota* = forced labor)
- *The Makropulos Affair*: immortality through a 300-year elixir — the ethical cost of eternal life
- *Krakatit*: an atomic phantasy about a weapon of absolute destructive power
- **Čapek's broader vision: robots, immortality, and atomic destruction**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, SF Encyclopedia, ISFDB, Encyclopedia.com

### Aldous Huxley: *Brave New World* (1932) — Bokanovsky's Process

- "Eighty-six identical twins working eighty-six identical machines" — the horror of biological mass production
- Sleep-teaching (hypnopaedia) to condition citizens into conformity
- The caste system from Alpha to Epsilon, biologically engineered for social control
- **The mass production of humanity: the assembly line applied to biology**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, EBSCO, Goodreads, GRIN

### Olaf Stapledon: *Last and First Men* (1930) — *Star Maker* (1937) — *Odd John* (1935)
- British philosopher and novelist (1886–1950), hugely influential on later SF
- *Last and First Men*: future history of 18 human species over 2 billion years, including telepathic and genetically engineered descendants
- *Star Maker*: a cosmic voyage through the history of the universe, encountering countless civilizations and their technological fates
- *Odd John*: a superhuman mutant with intelligence beyond normal humans, exploring the ethics of superior beings
- **The philosopher of cosmic scale: humanity's technological future across deep time**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, SF Encyclopedia, Hackwriters, Jeroenthoughts

- **The Christian technophobia: science as the tool of the demonic state**

### George Orwell: *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949)
- Telescreens: two-way televisions that watch citizens constantly
- Big Brother: the personification of surveillance and totalitarian control

### Ray Bradbury: *Fahrenheit 451* (1953)
- Firemen burn books in a dystopian America where technology suppresses dissent
- Mechanical Hound: a robotic dog that hunts and kills dissenters
- Parlor walls (immersive TV) and Seashells (earbuds) — technology as distraction and control
- **Technology as censorship: the burning of knowledge in the name of comfort**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, ResearchGate, Study.com, Facebook

### Arthur C. Clarke: *Childhood's End* (1953) — *The City and the Stars* (1956) — *The Sentinel* (1951)

- *The City and the Stars*: Diaspar — a computer-managed city where humans live for 1000 years, recreated by the Central Computer
- *The Sentinel* (1951): the short story that became the basis for *2001: A Space Odyssey*
- **Clarke's cosmic visions: transcendence through technology and the computer-managed utopia**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, EBSCO, Reactor Mag, Excelsior Forever

### Isaac Asimov: *Foundation* (1951) — *The Caves of Steel* (1954) — *The Naked Sun* (1957) — *The Bicentennial Man* (1976)
- *Foundation*: psychohistory — a mathematical science that predicts the future of human masses
- Hari Seldon predicts the fall of the Galactic Empire and engineers a 1000-year recovery
- *The Caves of Steel*: robots integrated into human society, detective Elijah Baley and robot Daneel Olivaw
- *The Naked Sun*: a society where humans have almost no physical contact, served by robots
- *The Bicentennial Man*: Andrew Martin, a robot, fights for 200 years to be recognized as human

### Clifford D. Simak: *City* (1952) — *Way Station* (1963)
- *City*: robots inherit Earth after humanity departs for the stars; the Webster family saga across millennia

- **The pastoral post-human: robots as the gentle inheritors of a vacant Earth**

### Fredric Brown: "Answer" (1954)
- American SF writer (1906–1972), master of the short short story
- All computers in the universe are linked to answer the question: "Is there a God?"
- The computer answers: "There is now." — and destroys the universe
- **The shortest AI apocalypse: the machine that becomes God**
- Quelle: Calum Chace, Schlock Value, Wikipedia

- *Second Variety*: autonomous killing machines ("claws") evolve beyond human control during a nuclear war
- *The Minority Report*: Precrime — a system that uses precognitive mutants to arrest people before they commit crimes
- *Ubik*: a reality-dissolving substance in a world where reality itself is manipulated by technology
- *Flow My Tears*: a police state where identity is fluid and technology controls who exists

- *Solaris*: an ocean planet that creates sentient simulacra from human memories — an alien intelligence that is fundamentally unknowable
- *The Invincible*: a swarm intelligence of self-evolving microscopic machines on a distant planet
- *Fiasco*: first contact with an alien civilization mediated by AI translation systems that may be failing
- *Golem XIV*: a superintelligence that refuses to serve humanity and instead philosophizes about the nature of consciousness
- *Peace on Earth*: an AI arms race on the Moon where machines have taken over warfare
- *Memoirs Found in a Bathtub*: a Kafkaesque bureaucratic dystopia of endless institutional paranoia
- *His Master's Voice*: scientists attempt to decode a message from space, but the "message" may be a biological technology
- **Lem's alien phenomenology: AI and intelligence that we cannot comprehend**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, SF Encyclopedia, Aeon, english.lem.pl, Reddit

- **The dice composer: chance operations in the Baroque era**

- **Haydn's algorithm: the classical era's generative music system**

- **The combinatorial organ: a 17th-century machine for composing music by rules**

- The first mobile robot with general reasoning capabilities
- Combined computer vision, natural language, and planning in a physical body
- **The first robot that "thought" while it moved: AI embodied**
- Quelle: SRI International, Wikipedia, AI Magazine

### Stanford Cart (1960s–1970s)
- Remote-controlled cart that navigated across a room in 5 hours (1979)
- Early computer vision: edge detection, path planning, obstacle avoidance
- **The slow birth of autonomous driving: 5 hours to cross a room**
- Quelle: Stanford, Wikipedia, Computer History Museum

### CYC Project (1984–present) — Douglas Lenat
- Cycorp's attempt to encode all human common sense in a knowledge base
- 25+ million hand-crafted assertions; the "encyclopedia of common sense"
- **The last stand of symbolic AI: can common sense be manually programmed?**
- Quelle: Cyc.com, Wikipedia, Medium, AlanTuring.net

### ALICE (1995) — Richard Wallace
- Artificial Linguistic Internet Computer Entity — AIML-based chatbot
- Won the Loebner Prize (Turing Test competition) three times (2000, 2001, 2004)
- **The open-source chatbot that defined the pre-LLM era**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, Codecademy, AIML Foundation

### Jabberwacky (1988) / Cleverbot (1997) — Rollo Carpenter
- Jabberwacky: learns from human conversations, no hardcoded responses
- Cleverbot: evolution of Jabberwacky, still online, millions of conversations
- **The social AI: learning from millions of human interactions**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, Cleverbot.com

### Deep Blue (1997) — IBM
- First computer to defeat a reigning world chess champion (Garry Kasparov)
- Brute-force search + evaluation functions — not "learning" but calculation
- **The victory of calculation over intuition: the machine that out-calculated humanity**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IBM, Facebook, The Refined Geek

### Watson (2011) — IBM
- Won Jeopardy! against champions Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter
- Natural language processing + massive knowledge base = "question answering"
- **The first AI that understood natural language well enough to beat trivia masters**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IBM, YouTube

### AlphaGo (2016) — DeepMind
- Defeated Lee Sedol, the world Go champion — a game thought too complex for brute force
- Monte Carlo Tree Search + deep neural networks + reinforcement learning
- **The AI that learned intuition: the moment "gut feeling" was mechanized**
- Quelle: Nature, DeepMind, Wikipedia

### AlphaZero (2017) — DeepMind
- Self-play learning: taught itself chess, shogi, and Go from scratch in 24 hours
- No human games used as training data — pure self-play reinforcement learning
- **The AI that became its own teacher: the loop of self-improvement**
- Quelle: Science, DeepMind, Wikipedia

### MuZero (2019) — DeepMind
- Model-based reinforcement learning: learns the rules of the game internally
- Masters games without being told the rules — builds its own model of reality
- **The AI that learns the laws of its universe: model-based reasoning**
- Quelle: Nature, DeepMind, Wikipedia

### GPT-1 (June 2018) → GPT-2 (Feb 2019) → GPT-3 (June 2020) → GPT-4 (March 2023)
- OpenAI's Generative Pre-trained Transformer series
- GPT-3: 175 billion parameters, emergent few-shot learning
- GPT-4: multimodal, reasoning, passes bar exams, medical boards
- **The LLM revolution: from text prediction to general reasoning**
- Quelle: OpenAI, arXiv, Towards Data Science, Reddit

### LaMDA (2021) / PaLM (2022) / Gemini (2023) — Google
- LaMDA: conversation-specialized model; sparked "sentience" debate (Blake Lemoine, 2022)
- PaLM: 540 billion parameters, breakthrough in reasoning and chain-of-thought
- Gemini: native multimodal, competing with GPT-4
- **Google's response to the LLM revolution: the search giant fights back**
- Quelle: Google AI Blog, arXiv, Wired, Washington Post

### Claude (2022–2025) — Anthropic
- Claude 1 (2022) → Claude 2 (2023) → Claude 3 (2024) → Claude 4 (2025)
- Constitutional AI: trained to be helpful, harmless, and honest through self-critique
- **The safety-first AI: built to refuse harmful requests by design**
- Quelle: Anthropic, arXiv, TechCrunch

### LLaMA (2023) / Mistral (2023) — Meta / Open Source Models
- LLaMA: Meta's open-weights model, leaked and sparked the open-source LLM movement
- Mistral: French startup, efficient small models that outperform larger ones
- **The open-source rebellion: when AI escaped the corporate labs**
- Quelle: Meta AI, arXiv, Hugging Face, Mistral.ai

## 10. AI in Art & Visual Culture

### Harold Cohen: AARON (1973–2016)
- Computer program that created original abstract art for over 40 years
- Started as line drawings, evolved to color, composition, and eventually physical robots
- **The longest-running AI art project: a machine that painted like a human for decades**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum, Medium (Nettrice Gaskins)

### Karl Sims: Evolved Virtual Creatures (1994)
- Genetic algorithms evolve virtual creatures that walk, swim, and compete
- Artificial evolution in simulated environments: survival of the fittest as art
- **The first evolutionary art: breeding creatures in the computer**
- Quelle: MIT Media Lab, Wikipedia, YouTube

### Scott Draves: Electric Sheep (1999–present)
- Distributed screen-saver that renders "sheep" (fractal animations) via collective computing
- 450,000+ computers participate; users vote on which sheep survive and reproduce
- **The first distributed AI art: millions of humans and machines co-creating**
- Quelle: electricsheep.org, MIT Press, Digital Art Database

### Refik Anadol: Data Sculpture (2016–present)
- Turkish-American artist who creates AI-generated "data sculptures" from archives
- "Unsupervised" at MoMA (2022): AI interpreting the museum's collection in real-time
- **The museum as AI canvas: machine dreams of human art**
- Quelle: MoMA, Wikipedia, Artsy, Hiscox Art and AI Report

### Obvious Art: "Edmond de Belamy" (2018)
- The first AI-generated artwork sold at a major auction house (Christie's, $432,500)
- Created by a Parisian collective using GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks)
- **The auction that made AI art legitimate: the Belamy moment**
- Quelle: Christie's, Wikipedia, CNN, Artsy, Artnome

### Mario Klingemann: Neural Network Art (2017–present)
- German artist who creates "memories of Passersby I" — a GAN that generates portraits in real-time
- Works with latent space, style transfer, and neural network aesthetics
- **The artist as curator of the machine unconscious: exploring latent space**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Artsy, Twitter/X, YouTube

### Sofia Crespo: Generative Biology Art (2018–present)
- Neural networks trained on biological forms to generate imaginary creatures
- "Neural Zoo" and "Artificial Natural History" — the aesthetics of synthetic biology
- **The AI that dreams of nature: generative biology as art practice**
- Quelle: Sofia Crespo website, Ars Electronica, Creative Applications

- **The first vision of AI as creative partner in architecture**

- Quelle: Wikipedia, ResearchGate, YouTube (ZHA + AI)

## 12. Military & Surveillance AI

### SAGE: Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (1958)
- US air defense system — the first large-scale computer network
- Real-time radar processing, automated threat assessment, human-in-the-loop
- **The first military AI: a computer network that watched the skies**
- Quelle: MIT Lincoln Laboratory, Wikipedia, History of Computer Communications

### ARPANET (1969)
- DARPA-funded network connecting research computers across the US
- The precursor to the Internet — packet switching, distributed communication
- **The network that became the world: military research → global infrastructure**
- Quelle: DARPA, Science Museum, Wikipedia

### ECHELON (1960s–present)
- Global signals intelligence network operated by Five Eyes (US, UK, Canada, Australia, NZ)
- Automated keyword scanning of global communications
- **The first AI surveillance: a machine that reads all the mail**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, European Parliament reports, The Guardian

### DARPA Grand Challenge (2004–2007)
- Autonomous vehicle race across the Mojave Desert — no human drivers
- Stanford's "Stanley" won the 2005 race, proving autonomous driving possible
- **The birth of self-driving cars: AI that conquered the desert**
- Quelle: DARPA, Wikipedia, Stanford

### X-47B (2013) — Northrop Grumman / US Navy
- First autonomous unmanned combat air vehicle (UCAV) to land on an aircraft carrier
- Can take off, fly, and land without human intervention
- **The autonomous weapon: a machine that decides to kill**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, US Navy, The Guardian

### Project Maven (2017–present)
- US military AI project for analyzing drone footage using computer vision
- Google employees protested, leading to Google's withdrawal and an ethical AI debate
- **The AI ethics rebellion: when engineers said no to military AI**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, The Guardian, TechCrunch, Google

### Palantir Technologies (2003–present)
- Data analytics platform founded by Peter Thiel, used by intelligence agencies and police
- Predictive policing, immigration enforcement, military operations
- **The surveillance capitalism-military complex: data as weapon**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Palantir, The Guardian, Business Insider

### Clearview AI (2017–present)
- Facial recognition company that scraped billions of photos from social media
- Used by law enforcement worldwide; banned in several European countries
- **The face-recognition panopticon: your face is a database entry**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, The New York Times, BuzzFeed, Wired

### Boston Dynamics: BigDog (2005) → Atlas (2013) → Spot (2016)
- BigDog: quadruped military robot for carrying gear over rough terrain
- Atlas: humanoid robot capable of parkour, backflips, and construction work
- Spot: commercial quadruped robot sold to police, industry, and military
- **The robot that walks: from military mule to industrial worker**
- Quelle: Boston Dynamics, Wikipedia, The Kid Should See This, Reddit

## 13. Feminist & Critical Perspectives on AI

### Donna Haraway: "A Cyborg Manifesto" (1985)
- "We are all cyborgs" — the blurring of boundaries between human, animal, and machine
- Rejects the "natural" body in favor of hybrid identities as political strategy
- **The feminist foundation of cybernetics: the cyborg as liberation**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, feminists@law, N+1 Magazine, YouTube

### Sadie Plant: Zeros + Ones (1997)
- Cyberfeminist manifesto: women and computers as allies against patriarchy
- Ada Lovelace as the "mother of software" — weaving as proto-programming
- **The feminist history of computing: Ada's daughters**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MIT Press, Cyberfeminism Archives

### N. Katherine Hayles: How We Became Posthuman (1999)
- The body is "the original prosthesis we all learn to manipulate"
- Information lost its body: the posthuman condition of disembodied data
- **The critique of AI disembodiment: what we lose when we become data**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, University of Chicago Press, Electronic Book Review

### Lucy Suchman: Plans and Situated Actions (1987)
- Human-machine communication fails because plans don't account for situated context
- The "situated action" critique: intelligence is always embodied and contextual
- **The anthropological critique of AI: you can't separate mind from body and place**
- Quelle: Cambridge University Press, Wikipedia, ResearchGate, Knight Columbia

### Sherry Turkle: Alone Together (2011) / Reclaiming Conversation (2015)
- How AI companions (robots, chatbots) change human relationships
- The "robotic moment": when we expect more from technology than from each other
- **The sociological warning: AI doesn't just replace labor, it replaces intimacy**
- Quelle: MIT, Wikipedia, The New York Times

### Simone Browne: Dark Matters (2015)
- "On the Surveillance of Blackness" — surveillance technologies as racializing tools
- The "digital epidermalization" of black bodies: how technology sees race
- **The race-critical lens on AI: surveillance is never neutral**
- Quelle: Duke University Press, Wikipedia, SSRN, Just Tech

### Ruha Benjamin: Race After Technology (2019)
- "The New Jim Code" — how technology reinvents racial hierarchies
- From medical devices to algorithms, technology encodes social bias
- **The abolitionist approach to technology: dismantling the coded color line**
- Quelle: Polity Press, Wikipedia, PMC, Algorithmic Justice League

### Safiya Noble: Algorithms of Oppression (2018)
- Google search results reinforce racism and sexism through algorithmic bias
- "The pornographic images that come up when searching 'Black girls'"
- **The search engine as racist archive: algorithms are not neutral**
- Quelle: NYU Press, Wikipedia, Algorithmic Justice League, Library Highline

### Joy Buolamwini: Gender Shades (2018) / Algorithmic Justice League (2016)
- MIT research showing facial recognition systems misidentify dark-skinned women at far higher rates
- Founded the Algorithmic Justice League to fight algorithmic bias
- **The poet of code: proving AI discrimination with data**
- Quelle: MIT Media Lab, Algorithmic Justice League, TED, Wikipedia

- **The labor apocalypse prediction: when machines do all the work**

- Technology and the Threat of a Jobless Future

- **The comprehensive job-loss thesis: no one is safe from the robots**

- Post-capitalism, accelerationism, and the demand for full automation
- "We demand full automation!" — the left-wing case for technological acceleration
- **The left accelerationist manifesto: speed up automation to abolish work**

- "A manifesto" — automation + renewable energy + AI = post-scarcity communism
- The "FALC" vision: machines do the work, humans enjoy the abundance
- **The utopian promise: AI as the engine of communist abundance**

- "Work, Progress, and Prosperity in a Time of Brilliant Technologies"

- China, Silicon Valley, and the New World Order
