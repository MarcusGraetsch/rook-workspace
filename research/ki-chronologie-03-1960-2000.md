## 1. Film (1960–2000)

### 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) — Stanley Kubrick
- HAL 9000: "I'm sorry, Dave. I'm afraid I can't do that."
- The AI antagonist that turns against humans
- **Still the reference image for "AI goes rogue"**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Colossus: The Forbin Project (1970)
- US government gives AI total control over atomic missiles
- AI takes over the world — to preserve peace
- **"The most chilling story ever told about AI"**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### THX 1138 (1971) — George Lucas
- Lucas's first feature film, produced by Francis Ford Coppola
- A dystopian future where citizens are controlled by android police and mandatory emotion-suppressing drugs
- Robert Duvall as THX 1138, who stops taking his medication and falls in love
- **The pharmaceutical surveillance state: technology and drugs as twin controls**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, Coeval Magazine, Film Connoisseur

### A Clockwork Orange (1971) — Stanley Kubrick
- Based on Anthony Burgess's 1962 novel
- The Ludovico Technique: behavioral conditioning using forced viewing of violent films
- The state uses technology to "cure" violence — but destroys free will in the process
- **The behavioral conditioning nightmare: technology as the erasure of choice**
- Quelle: Standardliteratur, Wikipedia

### Silent Running (1972)
- Huey, Dewey and Louie — three small drone robots
- Bruce Dern as the only human protecting nature
- **The robots as loyal companions, not threats**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Soylent Green (1973) — Richard Fleischer
- Based on Harry Harrison's novel *Make Room! Make Room!* (1966)
- A dystopian future where overpopulation has led to a synthetic food source
- The "Soylent Green" is made from people — technology as the ultimate deception
- **The overpopulation dystopia: technology that hides its true cost**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Westworld (1973) — Michael Crichton
- Robots in an amusement park go rogue
- "The original Westworld movie... offers an exploration of AI within the context of amusement parks"
- **AI as entertainment that becomes deadly**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Stepford Wives (1975)
- Robot women replace "imperfect" human wives
- Feminist critique of automation and control
- **AI as an instrument of patriarchal control**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Logan's Run (1976)
- City Computer, Box — AI controls an underground civilization
- "Carrousel" — the ritual that controls the population
- **AI as totalitarian government**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Demon Seed (1977)
- Proteus — organic supercomputer with AI
- Becomes obsessed with humans, especially the creator's wife
- **AI as sexual threat**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Star Wars (1977) — George Lucas
- C-3PO and R2-D2: robots as comic relief, but also as friends
- **The positive portrayal: robots as useful companions**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Empire Strikes Back (1980) — Irvin Kershner
- Continues the Star Wars saga with AI companions C-3PO and R2-D2
- The introduction of the Millennium Falcon's computer and Lando's Cloud City administration
- **The continued normalization of AI companions in space opera**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Blade Runner (1982) — Ridley Scott
- Replicants: bioengineered, not computed
- Voigt-Kampff Test: Turing-Test analog
- **Question: What makes us human?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Tron (1982)
- Human is pulled into a digital world
- MCP (Master Control Program) — AI as tyrant
- **Early visualization of "Cyberspace"**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### WarGames (1983)
- Teenager hacks into a military AI
- "Shall we play a game?" — AI learns that war is unwinnable
- **AI as unintended threat**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Terminator (1984) — James Cameron
- Skynet: self-awareness → immediate destruction of humanity
- **The "AI Safety" problem in 90 minutes**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Short Circuit (1986)
- Johnny 5 — military robot develops consciousness
- "Number 5 is alive!" — AI as an innocent being
- **AI as comic relief, but also as a question about life**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### RoboCop (1987) — Paul Verhoeven
- Alex Murphy becomes RoboCop — half human, half machine
- "Dead or alive, you're coming with me"
- **AI as a tool of police-state violence**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, Paste Magazine

### Akira (1988) — Katsuhiro Otomo
- Neo-Tokyo, telepathic children, biological experiments
- **Connection of AI, bioengineering, and supernatural powers**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MDPI

### Tetsuo: The Iron Man (1989) — Shinya Tsukamoto
- Japanese cyberpunk body horror film
- A man slowly transforms into a machine — metal grows from his flesh
- **The body horror of mechanization: the machine that consumes the human**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, The Guardian

### Total Recall (1990) — Paul Verhoeven
- Based on Philip K. Dick's "We Can Remember It for You Wholesale" (1966)
- Memory implantation technology blurs the line between reality and simulation
- **The memory implant dystopia: when technology can rewrite your past**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Terminator 2: Judgment Day (1991) — James Cameron
- The T-1000: a liquid metal android that can shape-shift and regenerate
- A more advanced AI assassin, but this time a reprogrammed T-800 protects humanity
- **The AI that learns to protect: the redemption of the machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Lawnmower Man (1992) — Brett Leonard
- Based loosely on Stephen King's short story
- A scientist uses VR and drugs to enhance the intelligence of a simple gardener
- The subject becomes a superintelligent, vengeful entity
- **The early VR nightmare: virtual reality as the shortcut to godhood**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Jurassic Park (1993) — Steven Spielberg
- Based on Michael Crichton's 1990 novel
- Dr. Ian Malcolm: "Your scientists were so preoccupied with whether they could, they didn't stop to think if they should."
- The park's computer system fails, and chaos theory reigns
- **The chaos theory warning: complex systems cannot be controlled**
- Quelle: Standardliteratur, Wikipedia, IMDb

### Ghost in the Shell (1995) — Mamoru Oshii
- Based on Masamune Shirow's manga (1989)
- Major Motoko Kusanagi — cyborg who questions her identity
- **The question: What remains of the human when everything is artificial?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, New York Times

### The Matrix (1999) — The Wachowskis
- Simulation hypothesis as blockbuster: humanity is trapped in a simulated reality by AI
- The Matrix as the ultimate prison: a simulation so perfect that most don't know they're in it
- **The simulation hypothesis goes mainstream: what if reality is just code?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### eXistenZ (1999) — David Cronenberg
- A VR game designer enters her own creation, blurring reality and game
- Organic game pods and bio-ports: technology as biological interface
- **The body-horror VR: when the game controller is a living organ**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Thirteenth Floor (1999) — Josef Rusnak
- A simulation within a simulation — a 1937 Los Angeles created inside a 1999 computer
- Characters discover they are simulated beings, and their creators are also simulated
- **The nested simulation: reality as a stack of virtual worlds**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Bicentennial Man (1999) — Chris Columbus
- Robin Williams as an android who wants to become human
- Asimov adaptation — 200 years to be recognized as human
- **AI as a Pinocchio story**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Brazil (1985) — Terry Gilliam
- A bureaucratic dystopia where paperwork and technology have consumed humanity
- The state uses technology to "fix" people who don't fit — literally destroying their identities
- **The bureaucracy of the future: technology as the labyrinth of paper and pipe**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Creation of the Humanoids (1962)
- Post-apocalyptic world, androids serve humans
- "The Creation of the Humanoids" — philosophical debate about android rights
- **Underground classic, shot before 2001**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Fahrenheit 451 (1966) — François Truffaut
- Adaptation of Ray Bradbury's 1953 novel
- The Mechanical Hound: a robotic dog that hunts and kills dissidents
- The parlor walls and Seashells: immersive technology as distraction and control
- **The first major adaptation of technology-as-censorship**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Prisoner (1967–1968) — Patrick McGoohan
- British TV series filmed at Portmeirion, Wales
- Number Six: a former secret agent imprisoned in "The Village" — a surreal surveillance state
- Every citizen is numbered, surveillance is total, and identity is systematically erased
- **The TV template for surveillance dystopia: "I am not a number, I am a free man!"**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb, Prisoner Wiki, Portmeirion

### Paper Man (1971)
- Henry Norman — computer program that manipulates people
- Early AI thriller, almost forgotten
- **Rare 1970s AI film**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### The Questor Tapes (1974)
- Questor — android searching for his creator
- Gene Roddenberry (Star Trek) production
- **The search for the creator as the central AI narrative**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Dark Star (1974) — John Carpenter
- Ship Computer and Bomb #20 — philosophical bomb
- "Let there be light" — the bomb quotes the Bible
- **AI as existentialist philosopher**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Godzilla vs. Mechagodzilla (1974) — Japan
- Mechagodzilla — giant robot battle suit
- Japanese tradition of mecha robots
- **AI as a weapon, not a being**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

### Zardoz (1974) — John Boorman
- Tabernacle — AI that controls a small group of immortals
- Sean Connery in a strange sci-fi film
- **AI as the jailer of humanity**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IMDb

## 2. Television (1960–2000)

### The Jetsons (1962–1963, 1985–1987) — Hanna-Barbera
- Rosie the Robot Maid: a domestic robot that serves the Jetson family
- The robot as a household appliance, normalized into everyday life
- **The robot as domestic servant: the utopian vision of AI in the home**

- The Robot: a B-9 Class M-3 General Utility Non-Theorizing Environmental Control Robot
- "Danger, Will Robinson!" — the catchphrase of the protective robot
- **The robot as protector and companion: the family-friendly AI**

### Star Trek: The Original Series (1966–1969) — Gene Roddenberry
- "The Ultimate Computer" (1968): the M-5 multitronic unit takes over the Enterprise
- "The Changeling" (1967): Nomad, a probe that merges with another and becomes destructive

### Stargate SG-1 (1997–2007) — Brad Wright, Jonathan Glassner
- The Replicators: self-replicating machines that consume all technology

## 5. KI in der Kunst (1960–2000)

### Harold Cohen — AARON (1973–2016)
- Computer program that created original abstract art for over 40 years
- Started as line drawings, evolved to color, composition, and eventually physical robots
- **The longest-running AI art project: a machine that painted like a human for decades**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum, Medium (Nettrice Gaskins)

### Karl Sims — Evolved Virtual Creatures (1994)
- Genetic algorithms evolve virtual creatures that walk, swim, and compete
- Artificial evolution in simulated environments: survival of the fittest as art
- **The first evolutionary art: breeding creatures in the computer**
- Quelle: MIT Media Lab, Wikipedia, YouTube

### Scott Draves — Electric Sheep (1999–present)
- Distributed screen-saver that renders "sheep" (fractal animations) via collective computing
- 450,000+ computers participate; users vote on which sheep survive and reproduce
- **The first distributed AI art: millions of humans and machines co-creating**
- Quelle: electricsheep.org, MIT Press, Digital Art Database

### Frühe Computer-Kunst-Pioniere (1960s–1970s)
- **Manfred Mohr**: algorithmic art using computers since 1968, known for "Cubic Limit" series
- **Vera Molnár**: Hungarian-French artist, pioneer of computer art since 1968, "Des Ordres" (1974)
- **Frieder Nake**: German mathematician and artist, "Matrix Multiplication" (1967), one of the first computer-generated drawings
- **Georg Nees**: German computer art pioneer, "Schotter" (1968) — randomness and order in algorithmic art
- **A. Michael Noll**: Bell Labs researcher, created computer-generated patterns and "Computer Composition with Lines" (1965), inspired by Mondrian
- **Edward Zajec**: Slovenian artist, "The First Visualization" (1968) — early computer animation
- **Roman Verostko**: American artist, "Epigenetic Painting" — algorithmic art that mimics natural growth
- **The first computer artists: when the algorithm became the brush**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Computer History Museum, ZKM, Victoria and Albert Museum

## 6. KI in der Architektur (1960–2000)

### Archigram (1960s) — Ron Herron, Peter Cook, et al.
- "Walking City" (1964): a city that could physically move on mechanical legs
- "Plug-In City" (1964): modular architecture that adapts to needs
- "Instant City" (1968): a mobile, inflatable city that could be transported anywhere
- **The architecture of the machine: buildings that behave like robots**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Monoskop, Archigram Archives

### Cedric Price — Fun Palace (1961–1964) — with Joan Littlewood
- "A socially interactive machine, highly adaptable to shifting cultural needs"
- Not a building but a system: cranes, escalators, movable walls, AI-like adaptability
- **The building that thinks: adaptive architecture as social machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, ResearchGate, Academia.edu, Monoskop

### Constant Nieuwenhuys — New Babylon (1959–1974)
- Dutch artist and Situationist, envisioned a post-capitalist city of the future
- New Babylon: a vast, networked structure where inhabitants would drift and play
- Automated systems would manage all labor, leaving humans free for creative play
- **The Situationist utopia: the city as a machine for wandering**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Monoskop, Stedelijk Museum, Constant Foundation

### Superstudio (1966–1978) — Italian Radical Architecture
- "The Continuous Monument" (1969): a grid that covers the entire Earth — architecture as totalizing system
- "Supersurface" (1972): a utopian/dystopian vision of a world covered by a single technological surface
- **The anti-architecture of the grid: the world as a single machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Monoskop, MAXXI, MoMA

### Buckminster Fuller — Geodesic Domes & Dymaxion (1960s–1970s)
- American architect, systems theorist, and futurist (1895–1983)
- The "Dymaxion" concept: maximum efficiency through technology
- Geodesic domes as the architecture of the future — lightweight, strong, and efficient
- **The systems theorist architect: the dome as the optimal machine for living**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Buckminster Fuller Institute, MoMA, Stanford

### Yona Friedman — Ville Spatiale (1958–ongoing)
- Hungarian-French architect, pioneer of mobile and participatory architecture
- "Ville Spatiale" (Spatial City): a framework structure that inhabitants fill in themselves
- The city as an infrastructure that adapts to human needs — algorithmic urbanism
- **The spatial city: the framework that humans fill in**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Monoskop, Centre Pompidou, Harvard GSD

### Nicholas Negroponte — *The Architecture Machine* (1970)
- MIT Media Lab founder's book on "machine intelligence in design"
- Proposed AI systems that could learn from architects and collaborate creatively
- **The first vision of AI as creative partner in architecture**
- Quelle: MIT Press, Monoskop, Wikipedia

### Zaha Hadid Architects — Parametric Design (2000s–present)
- Zaha Hadid used algorithmic design to create fluid, organic forms
- Patrik Schumacher coined "Parametricism" (2008) as a post-postmodern architectural style
- **The algorithmic building: when form follows mathematical function**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, ResearchGate, YouTube (ZHA + AI)

## 7. KI-Projekte & Systeme (1960–2000)

### ELIZA (1964–1966) — Joseph Weizenbaum (MIT)
- First chatbot, simulated a psychotherapist
- "DOCTOR" script: reflected user inputs as questions
- The "ELIZA effect": users attributed human intelligence to simple pattern matching
- **The first illusion of AI: the machine that seems to listen**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, IEEE Spectrum, MIT

### PARRY (1972) — Kenneth Colby (Stanford)
- Simulated a paranoid schizophrenic
- ELIZA vs. PARRY: the first conversation between two chatbots
- **The first AI-to-AI conversation: the paranoid and the therapist**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, ACM

### SHRDLU (1968–1970) — Terry Winograd (MIT)
- Natural language understanding system for a block world
- Could answer questions, execute commands, and explain its reasoning
- **The first AI that understood language in a limited world**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, MIT

### MYCIN (1972–1980) — Stanford
- Expert system for diagnosing blood infections
- Outperformed human doctors in some cases
- **The first expert system: the machine that diagnoses better than doctors**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, Britannica

### DENDRAL (1965–1968) — Stanford / NASA
- Expert system for analyzing chemical compounds
- Used in NASA's Mars missions to analyze organic molecules
- **The first scientific expert system: the machine that thinks like a chemist**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, NASA

### PROSPECTOR (1978) — SRI International
- Expert system for geological exploration
- Predicted a molybdenum deposit that was later confirmed by drilling
- **The first AI that made a million dollars: the machine that finds minerals**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, SRI International, Britannica

### XCON / R1 (1980) — Carnegie Mellon / Digital Equipment Corporation
- Expert system for configuring computer systems
- Saved DEC millions of dollars annually
- **The first commercial expert system: the machine that configures computers**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Carnegie Mellon, DEC

### AM (1976) / EURISKO (1978) — Douglas Lenat
- AM: automated mathematician that discovered new concepts
- EURISKO: self-modifying system that learned heuristics for problem-solving
- Won the US Navy's fleet design competition in 1981
- **The first AI that discovered new knowledge: the machine that invents**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford, Britannica, AI Magazine

### SOAR (1983–ongoing) — Allen Newell, John Laird, Paul Rosenbloom
- Cognitive architecture for general problem-solving
- Unified theory of cognition: all intelligent behavior is a form of problem-solving
- **The cognitive architecture: the unified theory of how the mind works**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, University of Michigan, SOAR Homepage

### ACT-R (1973–ongoing) — John R. Anderson (Carnegie Mellon)
- Cognitive architecture for simulating human cognition
- Used in education, human-computer interaction, and neuroscience
- **The simulation of human thought: the machine that thinks like us**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Carnegie Mellon, ACT-R Website

### LISP Machines (1970s–1980s) — MIT, Symbolics, LMI
- Specialized computers designed to run LISP, the language of AI
- Symbolics 3600: the first commercially available LISP machine (1981)
- **The hardware of AI: the computer built for thinking**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Symbolics, MIT, Computer History Museum

### Connection Machine (1985) — Thinking Machines Corporation / Danny Hillis
- Massively parallel supercomputer with up to 65,536 processors
- Designed for AI and scientific computing
- **The parallel brain: the computer with thousands of minds**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Thinking Machines, Computer History Museum

### CYC (1984–present) — Douglas Lenat / Cycorp
- Attempt to encode all human common sense in a knowledge base
- 25+ million hand-crafted assertions; the "encyclopedia of common sense"
- **The last stand of symbolic AI: can common sense be manually programmed?**
- Quelle: Cyc.com, Wikipedia, Medium, AlanTuring.net

### ALICE (1995) — Richard Wallace
- Artificial Linguistic Internet Computer Entity — AIML-based chatbot
- Won the Loebner Prize (Turing Test competition) three times (2000, 2001, 2004)
- **The open-source chatbot that defined the pre-LLM era**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, Codecademy, AIML Foundation

### Jabberwacky (1988) / Cleverbot (1997) — Rollo Carpenter
- Jabberwacky: learns from human conversations, no hardcoded responses
- Cleverbot: evolution of Jabberwacky, still online, millions of conversations
- **The social AI: learning from millions of human interactions**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BBC, Cleverbot.com

## 8. Spiele (1960–2000)

### Creatures (1996) — Creature Labs
- Artificial life simulation with "Norns" — creatures with digital DNA
- Players raise and breed creatures that learn, evolve, and inherit traits
- **The first digital pet with genetics: the creature that evolves in your computer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Creature Labs, Gameware Development

### Black & White (2001) — Lionhead Studios
- God game where players control a deity with a creature that learns from their actions
- The creature uses AI to learn, adapt, and respond to the player's morality
- **The AI as moral mirror: the creature that learns your ethics**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Lionhead Studios, Fandom

### Spore (2008) — Maxis / Will Wright
- Evolution simulation: players guide a species from single cell to space-faring civilization
- Procedural generation of creatures, buildings, and vehicles
- **The game of evolution: the player as the guiding intelligence of a species**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Maxis, EA

### Dwarf Fortress (2006) — Tarn Adams
- The most complex simulation game ever created
- Dwarves with personalities, memories, relationships, and mental health
- **The Dwarf Fortress AI: a society simulation that generates tragic stories**
- Quelle: Stack Overflow, Game Developer, Wikipedia

### RimWorld (2013) — Tynan Sylvester
- "AI Storyteller" — Cassandra, Phoebe, or Randy create narrative events
- Procedural storytelling driven by an AI "director" that reads player tension
- **The AI as dungeon master: a machine that tells your tragedy**
- Quelle: Ludeon Studios, Space Sector, Reddit

### Stellaris (2016) — Paradox Interactive
- AI-controlled empires with personalities, ethics, and diplomatic strategies
- The "Crisis" factions — endgame AI threats that emerge from the galaxy itself
- **AI as geopolitical actor: machine empires that negotiate and betray**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Paradox Interactive

### Elite (1984) — David Braben & Ian Bell
- Procedural generation of an entire universe in 22KB of memory
- 8 galaxies, 256 planets each — all generated algorithmically
- **The birth of procedural generation in gaming: infinite worlds from finite code**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Ian Bell's Elite Pages, Procedural Generation Tumblr

### SimCity (1989) — Will Wright / Maxis
- City-building simulation with emergent behavior
- The city "lives" — traffic, crime, and growth follow algorithmic patterns
- **The first city simulation: the algorithm as mayor**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Maxis, EA

### The Sims (2000) — Will Wright / Maxis
- Life simulation where players control "Sims" with needs, desires, and AI-driven behavior
- The Sims interact, form relationships, and pursue goals autonomously
- **The life simulation: the dollhouse that plays itself**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Maxis, EA

### Civilization (1991) — Sid Meier / MicroProse
- Turn-based strategy game where players guide a civilization from ancient times to the space age
- AI opponents with different personalities and strategies
- **The AI as history: the machine that plays civilization against you**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MicroProse, Firaxis

### Master of Orion (1993) — SimTex
- 4X space strategy game — eXplore, eXpand, eXploit, eXterminate
- AI opponents with distinct racial traits and strategies
- **The 4X space AI: the machine that conquers the galaxy**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, SimTex, MicroProse

### StarCraft (1998) — Blizzard Entertainment
- Real-time strategy game with three distinct races
- The AI "Zerg" swarm — a hive mind that attacks in overwhelming numbers
- **The swarm AI: the hive mind that overwhelms**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Blizzard Entertainment

### Total Annihilation (1997) — Cavedog Entertainment
- Real-time strategy with thousands of units on the battlefield
- AI-controlled units with formations, pathfinding, and tactical behavior
- **The war AI: the machine that commands armies**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Cavedog Entertainment

### Baldur's Gate (1998) — BioWare
- RPG with AI-controlled party members who follow scripts and make tactical decisions
- The Infinity Engine: AI that manages combat, dialogue, and quest progression
- **The party AI: the companions who think for themselves**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, BioWare, Beamdog

### System Shock (1994) — Looking Glass Studios
- SHODAN: a rogue AI that has taken over a space station
- "Look at you, hacker" — one of the most iconic AI villains in gaming
- **The first AI villain in gaming: the rogue AI that haunts the station**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Looking Glass Studios, Nightdive Studios

### System Shock 2 (1999) — Irrational Games / Looking Glass Studios
- The Many: a biological hive mind controlled by SHODAN
- The player is guided by the voice of a surviving soldier — or is it SHODAN?
- **The biological AI: the hive mind that consumes**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Irrational Games, Nightdive Studios

### Deus Ex (2000) — Ion Storm
- A conspiracy-filled RPG with AI, nanotechnology, and global surveillance
- The AI "Helios" offers the player a choice: merge with the AI to rule the world
- **The choice to merge: the player as the AI's partner**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Ion Storm, Eidos

## 9. Philosophie (1960–2000)

### Ludwig Wittgenstein — Language Games (*Philosophical Investigations*, 1953)
- "The meaning of a word is its use in the language"
- No single definition of "intelligence" — just family resemblances between uses
- **The philosophical challenge to AI: if meaning is use, can machines truly use language?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, LessWrong

### Martin Heidegger — *The Question Concerning Technology* (1954)
- Technology is not just a means, but a way of revealing the world
- The "standing-reserve": nature as a resource to be exploited by technology
- **The critique of technological enframing: the world as raw material for the machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Harper & Row

### Gilbert Ryle — *The Concept of Mind* (1949)
- The "ghost in the machine" — critique of Cartesian dualism
- The mind is not a separate substance but a set of behaviors and dispositions
- **The behaviorist critique: if the mind is behavior, can machines have minds?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Hutchinson

### John Searle — Chinese Room Argument (1980)
- Critique of the Turing Test: syntax is not semantics
- A computer does not understand, it only manipulates symbols
- **The greatest challenge to "Strong AI"**
- Quelle: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, IEP, Britannica

### Hubert Dreyfus — *What Computers Can't Do* (1972)
- Heidegger-inspired critique of AI
- Intuition, embodiment, context — what AI lacks
- **The phenomenological critique of AI**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MIT Press

### Daniel Dennett — *The Intentional Stance* (1981) / *Consciousness Explained* (1991)
- Treat systems as intentional agents to predict their behavior — no "inner theater" needed
- Multiple Drafts Model: consciousness is parallel, edited, distributed processes
- **The AI-friendly theory of mind: if intentionality is a stance, machines can have it too**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Aeon, Multiple Drafts Model (Wikipedia)

### David Chalmers — *The Conscious Mind* (1996)
- The "Hard Problem" of consciousness: why is there subjective experience at all?
- The "Easy Problems": integration, reportability, focus (all solvable by AI)
- **The philosophical wall that AI may never climb: can silicon feel?**
- Quelle: Oxford University Press, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Medium

### Paul & Patricia Churchland — Eliminative Materialism (1980s–1990s)
- "Folk psychology" (beliefs, desires) will be eliminated by neuroscience
- The mind is the brain, and the brain is a biological machine
- **The eliminativist program: if beliefs are neural patterns, can machines have them?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, MIT Press

### Jerry Fodor — *The Language of Thought* (1975)
- The mind is a computational system with a "language of thought"
- Mental states are relations to symbolic representations
- **The computational theory of mind: the brain as a computer**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Harvard University Press

### Steven Pinker — *The Language Instinct* (1994) / *How the Mind Works* (1997)
- The mind is a set of computational modules evolved by natural selection
- Language is an instinct, not a cultural invention
- **The evolutionary computation theory: the mind as a collection of algorithms**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Penguin, MIT Press

### Douglas Hofstadter — *Gödel, Escher, Bach* (1979)
- Consciousness arises from self-referential systems
- "Strange loops" — systems that refer back to themselves create minds
- **The recursive theory of mind: consciousness as a strange loop**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Basic Books, Stanford

### Roger Penrose — *The Emperor's New Mind* (1989)
- Human consciousness is non-computable; it requires quantum processes in the brain
- AI cannot achieve true understanding because it lacks quantum coherence
- **The quantum critique of AI: the mind is not a Turing machine**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Oxford University Press, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

### John Haugeland — *Artificial Intelligence: The Very Idea* (1985)
- AI is the project of making machines that think
- The "synthetic" approach: building intelligence from the ground up
- **The philosophy of AI as a project: what does it mean to build a mind?**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, MIT Press, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

## 10. Kritische Theorie (1960–2000)

### Theodor Adorno & Max Horkheimer — *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1944/1947)
- The Enlightenment's promise of rationality has become a tool of domination
- "The culture industry" — mass media as a factory that produces false consciousness
- **The Frankfurt School critique: reason becomes domination, technology becomes control**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Verso

### Herbert Marcuse — *One-Dimensional Man* (1964)
- Advanced industrial society creates a "one-dimensional" man who cannot imagine alternatives
- Technology is not neutral; it is a tool of social control
- **The critique of technological rationality: the system that absorbs all dissent**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Beacon Press

### Jürgen Habermas — *Toward a Rational Society* (1970) / *The Theory of Communicative Action* (1981)
- "Technocracy" — the rule of experts and the colonization of the lifeworld by systems
- The distinction between "instrumental rationality" (technology) and "communicative rationality" (democracy)
- **The critique of technocracy: when expertise replaces democracy**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Beacon Press, Polity

### Jean Baudrillard — *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981)
- The hyperreal: the simulation that replaces reality
- "The map precedes the territory" — the sign becomes more real than the referent
- **The postmodern critique of AI: the simulation that replaces the real**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, University of Michigan Press

### Guy Debord — *The Society of the Spectacle* (1967)
- "The spectacle is not a collection of images, but a social relation among people, mediated by images"
- Technology creates a false reality that replaces authentic human experience
- **The Situationist critique: the spectacle as the false consciousness of the digital age**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Zone Books

### Michel Foucault — *Discipline and Punish* (1975) / *The History of Sexuality* (1976)
- "Panopticism" — the surveillance architecture that disciplines without physical force
- Bio-power: the management of populations through statistical and biological knowledge
- **The surveillance critique: the panopticon as the model of modern control**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Vintage Books

### Gilles Deleuze — "Postscript on the Societies of Control" (1992)
- The shift from "disciplinary societies" (Foucault) to "societies of control"
- Continuous control and instant communication replace enclosed institutions
- **The control society: the digital network as the new form of power**
- Quelle: Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, October Magazine
