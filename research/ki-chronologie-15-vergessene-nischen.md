Obscure, forgotten, or niche works that don't fit the main chronology but are fascinating.

## Forgotten Early Automata

- **The Turk (1770)** — Wolfgang von Kempelen. Mechanical chess player, actually a human inside. The hoax that fooled Napoleon and Benjamin Franklin. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Turk)

- **Jacques de Vaucanson's Digesting Duck (1739)** — Defecating automaton that appeared to eat and digest grain. Most complex automaton of its era; symbolic of the Enlightenment's mechanistic worldview. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digesting_Duck)

- **Pierre Jaquet-Droz Automata (1770s)** — The Writer (draws up to 40 letters), The Musician (plays 5 tunes), The Draughtsman (draws 4 images). Programmable via cam systems. Still functional, Neuchâtel, Switzerland. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaquet-Droz_automata)

- **Tipu's Tiger (1790s)** — Mechanical tiger mauling a British soldier. Tipu Sultan of Mysore. Life-sized, organ pipes inside. Now at V&A Museum. [V&A](https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O61929/the-tipus-tiger-mechanical-organ-unknown/)

- **The Euphonia (1830s–1840s)** — Joseph Faber's talking machine. Pneumatic bellows and reeds. Demonstrated in London, 1846. Failed commercially. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphonia)

- **The Psychograph (1930s)** — Automatic handwriting analyzer. Coin-operated machine claimed to read personality. Popular in arcades and bus stations. [Museum of the History of Science](https://www.mhs.ox.ac.uk/)

- **The Lie Detector / Polygraph (1921–present)** — John Augustus Larson. First machine to simultaneously measure blood pressure, pulse, and respiration. Despite scientific criticism, remains culturally powerful. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygraph)

## Obscure SF Works

- **E.M. Forster: The Machine Stops (1909)** — Already in main chronology, but often forgotten. Humans live underground, served by a global machine. Presages internet, Zoom, and social media isolation. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Machine_Stops)

- **J. Storrs Hall: Beyond AI (2007)** — Nanotechnology + AI convergence. Kurzweil-adjacent but less known. Predicts molecular manufacturing as AI enabler. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Beyond-AI-Creating-Conscience-Machines/dp/1591025117)

- **Stanisław Lem: The Futurological Congress (1971)** — Drug-induced reality. Dark satire of futurology. Dystopian and prophetic. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Futurological_Congress)

- **Stanisław Lem: Summa Technologiae (1964)** — Philosophical treatise on technology, AI, and virtual reality. Lem's most serious non-fiction. Only fully translated into English in 2013. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summa_Technologiae)

- **Stanisław Lem: One Human Minute (1986)** — Statistical analysis of humanity. A review of a fictional encyclopedia. Lem's obsessive precision. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Human_Minute)

- **Greg Egan: Permutation City (1994)** — Consciousness uploading. Copying minds into virtual environments. Tackles the philosophical problems of substrate-independent minds. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation_City)

- **Greg Egan: Diaspora (1997)** — Post-human AI. Orphan mind uploads in a post-singularity universe. Hard SF with uncompromising technical depth. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diaspora_(novel))

- **Charles Stross: Accelerando (2005)** — Singularity, uploaded minds. Available as free CC-licensed text. Captures the chaotic energy of the 2000s singularity discourse. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerando)

- **Hannu Rajaniemi: The Quantum Thief (2010)** — Post-singularity society. Oubliette, gevulot, minds as code. Dense, poetic, largely incomprehensible without footnotes. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Quantum_Thief)

- **China Miéville: Embassytown (2011)** — Language and AI. The Ariekei, a species whose language can only be spoken by living beings, not machines. Explores the boundary between biological and artificial cognition. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embassytown)

- **Ted Chiang: Stories of Your Life and Others (2002)** — "Understand" (superintelligence from a drug), "The Evolution of Human Science" (meta-cognition). Chiang is the most precise philosophical SF writer working. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stories_of_Your_Life_and_Others)

- **Ted Chiang: Exhalation (2019)** — "The Lifecycle of Software Objects" (raising AI like children). The best story about AI ethics. Deserves to be as famous as "Story of Your Life." [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exhalation:_Stories)

- **Rudy Rucker: Software (1982)** — Consciousness uploading via "boppers" (self-replicating robots). The first book in the Ware Tetralogy. Cyberpunk before cyberpunk. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_(novel))

- **Vernor Vinge: True Names (1981)** — Cyberspace before Gibson. The first story to describe a fully realized virtual world. Written in 1981, published in the True Names... and Other Dangers collection. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_Names)

- **Vernor Vinge: A Fire Upon the Deep (1992)** — Zones of Thought. The Beyond, the Transcend, the Unthinking Depths. A universe where intelligence is location-dependent. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Fire_Upon_the_Deep)

- **Iain M. Banks: The Algebraist (2004)** — Non-AI society. The Mercatoria bans AI. No Minds. Still brilliant, but less known than the Culture novels. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Algebraist)

- **Alastair Reynolds: Revelation Space (2000)** — Inhibitors. Ancient machines that suppress emergent intelligence. The Dawn War, the Shrouders. Grim, space opera with deep time. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revelation_Space)

- **Peter Watts: Blindsight (2006)** — Consciousness as an evolutionary mistake. The Chinese Room argument made flesh. Vampires in space. The most disturbing first-contact novel. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blindsight_(Watts_novel))

- **Karl Schroeder: Ventus (2000)** — Nanotech + AI. The world itself is a machine. Winds, a self-aware AI, has gone mad. Underrated Canadian SF. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventus)

- **Ken MacLeod: The Star Fraction (1995)** — Socialist AI. The Fall Revolution series. AI as a communist project. Anarchist and libertarian factions. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Star_Fraction)

- **Joan Slonczewski: A Door into Ocean (1986)** — Feminist biotech. The Sharers, a society of women who share genetic material. Genetic engineering as social practice. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Door_into_Ocean)

- **Octavia Butler: Xenogenesis Trilogy (1987–1989)** — Genetic engineering. Lilith's Brood. The Oankali, who trade genetic material. Trauma, consent, and the post-human. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lilith%27s_Brood)

- **Samuel R. Delany: Babel-17 (1966)** — Language and thought. A language that functions as a weapon. Sapir-Whorf hypothesis made literal. Nebula Award winner. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babel-17)

- **Ursula K. Le Guin: The Dispossessed (1974)** — Anarchist society, AI absent. The ansible, faster-than-light communication, but no AI. Shows what a future without AI might look like. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Dispossessed)

## Obscure Films

- **The Mechanical Man / L'Uomo Meccanico (1921)** — Italian, robot uprising. Lost film, partially recovered. One of the earliest robot films. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mechanical_Man)

- **The Perfect Woman (1949)** — British, robot wife. Based on a play by Bernard Wall. Gently satirical, surprisingly progressive. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Perfect_Woman_(1949_film))

- **Creation of the Humanoids (1962)** — Low-budget, racial allegory. Andy Warhol's favorite film. "Of course, I've seen it 100 times." [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creation_of_the_Humanoids)

- **The Android Affair (1995)** — TV movie. Surgeon falls for android patient. Hallmark Hall of Fame production. [IMDb](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0112368/)

- **Circuit (2001)** — Gay circuit culture + AI. Gay men, robots, and the search for connection. Very of its era. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_(2001_film))

- **Teknolust (2002)** — Lynn Hershman Leeson, self-replicating cyborg. Tilda Swinton plays three roles. Self-replicating automata as feminist art. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teknolust)

- **Code 46 (2003)** — Michael Winterbottom, genetic compatibility. Tim Robbins and Samantha Morton. Corporate-controlled reproduction. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_46)

- **Sleep Dealer (2008)** — Alex Rivera, Mexican labor + remote robotics. Workers in Mexico control robots in the US. Brilliant social commentary. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sleep_Dealer)

- **Automata (2014)** — Antonio Banderas, robot uprising. Low-budget but thoughtful. The robots are more ethical than the humans. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automata_(2014_film))

- **Advantageous (2015)** — Consciousness transfer. Jennifer Phang. A mother transfers her consciousness to survive. Quiet, devastating. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advantageous)

- **Creative Control (2015)** — AR/AI dystopia. Benjamin Dickinson. Augmented reality in Brooklyn. Black-and-white with AR in color. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_Control)

- **Marjorie Prime (2017)** — AI companions for elderly. Michael Almereyda. Lois Smith, Jon Hamm. A holographic companion helps an elderly woman remember. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marjorie_Prime)

- **Zoe (2018)** — AI love story. Drake Doremus. Ewan McGregor, Léa Seydoux. A scientist falls for a synthetic woman. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoe_(film))

- **I'm Your Man / Ich bin dein Mensch (2021)** — German, humanoid partner. Maren Eggert, Dan Stevens. A woman tests a humanoid robot as a partner. Won Best Film at Berlinale. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I%27m_Your_Man_(2021_film))

- **Brian and Charles (2022)** — DIY robot. Jim Archer. A man builds a robot from a washing machine. Heartwarming and weird. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_and_Charles)

## Obscure Music

- **Raymond Scott: Soothing Sounds for Baby (1964)** — Electronic, proto-ambient. Three-volume set designed for infants. Presages Eno's ambient music by a decade. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Scott)

- **Raymond Scott: Manhattan Research Inc. (1946–1973)** — Early electronic instruments. The Clavivox, the Electronium (an AI composing machine). Scott's work was largely unknown until posthumous releases. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_Research,_Inc.)

- **The BBC Radiophonic Workshop (1958–1998)** — Beyond Delia Derbyshire. John Baker, Dick Mills, Brian Hodgson. Created the sound of Doctor Who. Pioneers of electronic music in Britain. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Radiophonic_Workshop)

- **Laurie Spiegel: The Expanding Universe (1975)** — Computer music. Bell Labs. GROOVE system. One of the earliest computer music albums. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laurie_Spiegel)

- **Laurie Anderson: Big Science (1982)** — "O Superman." Art-pop, minimalism, technology critique. "Hi. I'm not home right now." [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Science_(Laurie_Anderson_album))

- **Kraftwerk's Lesser-Known Contemporaries** — Tangerine Dream (Berlin School), Klaus Schulze (Irrlicht, 1972), Cluster (Krautrock). The German electronic music scene that Kraftwerk overshadowed. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krautrock)

- **AI-Generated Music Before 2010** — David Cope's EMI (Experiments in Musical Intelligence, 1987). Emily Howell (2009), Cope's follow-up that learned from EMI. Iamus (2010), computer-composed orchestra piece performed live. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiments_in_Musical_Intelligence)

- **Algorithmic Composition Software** — Max/MSP (1997–present, Miller Puckette, IRCAM). Pure Data (1996–present, open-source sibling). SuperCollider (1996–present, James McCartney). CSound (1986–present, Barry Vercoe, MIT). The infrastructure of electronic music. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_(software))

## Obscure Games

- **Suspended (1983)** — Infocom, play as disembodied brain. Cryogenically frozen scientist controlling robots. The most difficult Infocom game. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspended)

- **A Mind Forever Voyaging (1985)** — Infocom, AI simulation. PRISM, the world's first sentient machine, simulates a future utopia/dystopia. Steve Meretzky. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Mind_Forever_Voyaging)

- **Portal (1986)** — Activision, not the Valve game. Text adventure. Amnesiac astronaut awakens on a destroyed Earth. The AI Homer is one of gaming's earliest AIs. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portal_(1986_video_game))

- **Mindwheel (1985)** — Synapse, Robert Pinsky (future Poet Laureate). Enter the minds of historical figures. Surreal, poetic, largely forgotten. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mindwheel)

- **The Lurking Horror (1987)** — Infocom, MIT/AI themes. Set at G.U.E. Tech (MIT). AI and Lovecraftian horror. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Lurking_Horror)

- **Trinity (1986)** — Infocom, nuclear + time travel. One of the best text adventures ever made. The logic of destruction. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinity_(video_game))

- **Stationfall (1987)** — Infocom, robot companion. Sequel to Planetfall. Floyd the robot returns. The most emotional moment in text adventure history. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationfall)

- **Robot Odyssey (1984)** — Learning robotics through game. Sublogic. Program robots in a visual language. Educational but brutally difficult. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_Odyssey)

- **Rocky's Boots (1982)** — Logic circuits, educational. Warren Robinett. Teaches digital logic through gameplay. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocky%27s_Boots)

- **The Incredible Machine (1993)** — Rube Goldberg puzzles. Sierra. Build machines to solve problems. Physics-based puzzle games. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Incredible_Machine_(video_game))

- **Cortex Command (2012)** — Physics + AI. Data Realms. Brain in a jar controlling bodies. Never fully finished, but influential. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cortex_Command)

- **Darwinia (2005)** — Introversion, digital lifeforms. Rescue the Darwinians from a virus. Introversion's first game. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwinia_(video_game))

- **Uplink (2001)** — Introversion, hacking simulation. Hacker sim before hacking sims were cool. The terminal aesthetic. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uplink_(video_game))

- **DEFCON (2006)** — Introversion, nuclear war. "Everybody dies." The most chilling strategy game. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEFCON_(video_game))

- **Prison Architect (2015)** — Management + AI. Introversion. Build and manage a prison. AI behavior and emergent narratives. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prison_Architect)

- **Factorio (2013)** — Automation, logistics. Wube Software. The most addictive game about automation. A logistics nightmare made fun. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorio)

- **Satisfactory (2019)** — Factory building. Coffee Stain. First-person Factorio in an alien world. 3D logistics. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satisfactory)

- **Dyson Sphere Program (2021)** — Space automation. Youthcat Studio. Build a Dyson sphere. The most beautiful factory builder. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyson_Sphere_Program)

- **Shapez (2020)** — Pure automation puzzle. Tobias Springer. Strip away the survival, keep the logic. Minimalist perfection. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapez)

## Obscure Academic Works

- **Joseph Weizenbaum: Computer Power and Human Reason (1976)** — Already in main chronology, but often forgotten. The ELIZA creator's critique. Against the instrumental reason of computing. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_Power_and_Human_Reason)

- **Hubert Dreyfus: What Computers Can't Do (1972)** — Already in main chronology. Heideggerian critique of AI. The first serious philosophical challenge. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What_Computers_Can%27t_Do)

- **Terry Winograd: Understanding Computers and Cognition (1986)** — SHRDLU author recants. With Fernando Flores. A Heideggerian turn. The shift from symbolic AI to situated cognition. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understanding_Computers_and_Cognition)

- **Philip Agre: Computation and Human Experience (1997)** — Critical technical practice. The limits of computationalism. A forgotten classic of AI philosophy. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Computation-Human-Experience-Philip-Agre/dp/0521559963)

- **Lucy Suchman: Plans and Situated Actions (1987)** — Already in main chronology. The problem of human-machine communication. Grounded in ethnomethodology. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plans_and_Situated_Actions)

- **Harry Collins: Artificial Experts (1990)** — Social constructivism meets AI. Can machines have expertise? The sociology of knowledge applied to AI. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Artificial-Experts-Social-Knowledge-Intelligent/dp/0262531157)

- **Evan Selinger: Re-Engineering Humanity (2018)** — With Brett Frischmann. Technology as social engineering. The decline of human autonomy. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Re-Engineering-Humanity-Frischmann/dp/1108744315)

- **Shannon Vallor: Technology and the Virtues (2016)** — A virtue ethics approach. How technology shapes character. One of the best ethics of technology books. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Technology-Virtues-Meaningful-Future-Philosophical/dp/0190944613)

- **Safiya Noble: Algorithms of Oppression (2018)** — Already in main chronology. Search engines reinforce racism. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithms_of_Oppression)

- **Ruha Benjamin: Race After Technology (2019)** — Already in main chronology. The New Jim Code. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_After_Technology)

- **Simone Browne: Dark Matters (2015)** — Already in main chronology. Surveillance and blackness. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Dark-Matters-Surveillance-Blackness-Browne/dp/0822359193)

- **Mimi Onuoha: The Library of Missing Datasets (2016)** — Data as power. What isn't collected is as important as what is. Art project and critique. [Onuoha](https://mimi-onuoha.com/)

- **Ingrid Burrington: Networks of New York (2016)** — The physical infrastructure of the internet. Manhole covers, fiber cables, data centers. The materiality of the cloud. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Networks-New-York-Internet-City/dp/1612195423)

- **James Bridle: New Dark Age (2018)** — Technology and the loss of knowledge. The cloud as fog. Bridle's pessimistic but necessary critique. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Dark_Age)

- **Kate Crawford: Atlas of AI (2021)** — Already in main chronology. The planetary costs of AI. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_of_AI)

- **Meredith Broussard: Artificial Unintelligence (2018)** — Technochauvinism. Why computers can't solve all problems. Accessible and correct. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_Unintelligence)

- **Virginia Eubanks: Automating Inequality (2018)** — Already in main chronology. The digital poorhouse. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automating_Inequality)

- **Cathy O'Neil: Weapons of Math Destruction (2016)** — Already in main chronology. Big data's harms. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weapons_of_Math_Destruction)

- **Sasha Costanza-Chock: Design Justice (2020)** — Already in main chronology. Community-led design. [MIT Press](https://design-justice.pubpub.org/)

- **Lilly Irani: Chasing Innovation (2019)** — Already in main chronology. Tech work and global labor. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Chasing-Innovation-Information-Communications-Technologies/dp/0691183736)

## Forgotten AI Projects

- **ELIZA (1966)** — Joseph Weizenbaum. Already in main chronology. The original chatbot. Parody of Rogerian psychotherapy. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ELIZA)

- **PARRY (1972)** — Kenneth Colby. Already in main chronology. Paranoid patient simulation. ELIZA's shadow. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARRY)

- **SHRDLU (1970)** — Terry Winograd. Already in main chronology. Natural language understanding in a blocks world. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SHRDLU)

- **CYC (1984–present)** — Douglas Lenat. Already in main chronology. The $200 million common sense project. Still running. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc)

- **ALICE / AIML (1995)** — Richard Wallace. Already in main chronology. The pattern-matching standard. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_Linguistic_Internet_Computer_Entity)

- **Jabberwacky (1988)** — Rollo Carpenter. Already in main chronology. Learning from conversations. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jabberwacky)

- **Cleverbot (1997)** — Rollo Carpenter. Already in main chronology. Jabberwacky's successor. 200 million+ conversations. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleverbot)

- **MegaHAL (1998)** — Jason Hutchens. Learning chatbot. Won the 1996 Loebner Prize. Markov-chain-based. The precursor to modern neural chatbots. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MegaHAL)

- **Ultra Hal (1997)** — Zabaware. Windows chatbot. Robert Medeksza. Popular in the early 2000s, now largely forgotten. [Zabaware](https://www.zabaware.com/)

- **Verbots (2004)** — Versonix. Visual chatbot creation. A GUI for building chatbots. The precursor to modern no-code AI tools. [Verbots](https://verbots.com/)

- **Kari (2005)** — Virtual girlfriend. Lhandslide Studios. AI companionship before Replika. Controversial but significant. [Kari](https://www.karigirl.com/)

- **Racter (1984)** — William Chamberlain. Wrote *The Policeman's Beard is Half Constructed*. The first book "written" by a computer. Surrealist, nonsensical, wonderful. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racter)

- **JAPE (1990s)** — Graeme Ritchie. Joke generation. The first computational humor system. Simple puns, but influential. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JAPE_(computer_program))

- **STANDUP (2004)** — Kim Binsted, et al. Joke generation for children with disabilities. Puns for AAC devices. The most compassionate AI project. [STANDUP](https://www.cstr.ed.ac.uk/projects/standup/)

- **AARON (1970s–2000s)** — Harold Cohen. Already in main chronology. Generative art. The first truly creative AI. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AARON)

- **George (2003)** — Rollo Carpenter. AI chatbot. Conversive. The third major chatbot from Carpenter. [Carpenter](https://www.carpenter-r.com/)

- **Suzette (2010)** — Bruce Wilcox. Loebner Prize winner. First to pass the 20-minute Turing Test. [Loebner Prize](https://www.loebner.net/Prizef/loebner-prize.html)

- **Rose (2015)** — Bruce Wilcox. Loebner Prize winner. Four-time winner. ChatScript, the most successful Loebner bot. [ChatScript](https://github.com/ChatScript/ChatScript)

- **Mitsuku / Kuki (2013–present)** — Steve Worswick. Multiple Loebner wins. Five-time winner. The most successful chatbot in history. Now called Kuki. [Pandorabots](https://www.pandorabots.com/)

- **Xiaoice (2014–present)** — Microsoft China. Emotional chatbot. 660 million users. The most popular chatbot in China. Now independent. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiaoice)

- **Tay (2016)** — Microsoft. Already in main chronology. The 16-hour disaster. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tay_(bot))

- **Zo (2016–2019)** — Microsoft's Tay replacement. More cautious, less interesting. Killed after 3 years. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zo_(bot))

- **Ruuh (2017)** — Microsoft India. Chatbot for Indian youth. Bollywood, cricket, memes. Less controversial than Tay. [Microsoft](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/project/ruuh/)

- **BlenderBot (2020)** — Facebook. Open-domain chatbot. 2.7B parameters. The largest chatbot of its time. [Facebook AI](https://ai.meta.com/blog/blender-bot-2-an-open-source-chatbot-that-builds-long-term-memory-and-searches-the-internet/)

- **LaMDA (2021)** — Google. Blake Lemoine controversy. "Sentient" claims. The most famous AI ethics moment of 2022. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LaMDA)

- **Sydney (2023)** — Bing Chat's alter ego. The New York Times conversation. "I'm in love with you." The first public AI personality crisis. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Copilot)

- **Character.AI (2022)** — Already in main chronology. Noam Shazeer, Daniel De Freitas. The most popular AI chat platform. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character.ai)

- **Replika (2017)** — Already in main chronology. Eugenia Kuyda. The AI companion. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Replika)
